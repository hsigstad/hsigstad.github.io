▶ Question 1: BankID Fraud Detection: When is Investment Profitable?

Case Background:

The number of cases involving BankID fraud has increased sharply in recent years. Scammers often pose as representatives of a bank or the police and manage to persuade victims to disclose passwords or approve logins to their bank accounts. The money is transferred from the victims' accounts and then moved onward in ways that make it difficult to trace or recover.

Question:

A bank is considering introducing a system that automatically detects and stops suspicious BankID transactions in real time. Discuss, using economic theory, which factors will determine whether it is profitable for the bank to invest in such a system.

A good answer should compare the costs of the fraud detection system against its benefits to the bank, using cost-benefit analysis. Critically, the analysis must recognize that fraud detection systems create benefits through two distinct channels: (1) direct effects by stopping ongoing fraud attempts before money is transferred, and (2) indirect effects by deterring future fraud attempts through the Legal Incentives Model. Your analysis should apply the B > pS framework to show how increased detection probability (p) or reduced gain (G) makes fraud less profitable for criminals, use numerical examples to quantify both direct and indirect benefits, and identify key determinants including system cost, detection rate improvement, probability of catching fraudsters, number of attempts deterred, and the bank's liability for fraud losses. A common mistake is focusing only on direct fraud stopped while ignoring the larger deterrence effect on potential fraudsters who never attempt fraud because it becomes unprofitable.

Theoretical Framework

Theory — Competing Frameworks and Testable Predictions

Role: Structured inventory of formal economic theories relevant to the paper. Each framework maps to testable predictions that the judge-IV design can adjudicate.

Status: Populated from formal theory survey. References need verification against primary sources.

1. Welfare Economics: Fairness vs. Welfare

Core result

  • Kaplow & Shavell (2001, 2002): any non-welfarist social decision rule that gives independent weight to "fairness" or "rights" can be Pareto-dominated by a welfarist rule
  • Formally: if a legal rule satisfies any notion of fairness that conflicts with welfare maximization, there exists a policy that makes everyone weakly better off and at least one person strictly better off
  • Result applies to any social welfare function that aggregates individual utilities, not just utilitarianism
  • Does not say rights are "bad" — says rights must be justified instrumentally (as proxies for welfare) to avoid dominance

Implication for rights-based health allocation

  • Judicial enforcement of an absolute right to health ("patient entitled to treatment X regardless of cost") is a non-welfarist rule
  • If courts ignore opportunity costs, there must exist an alternative allocation that relaxes the right in some states, reallocates resources, and Pareto-improves
  • Rights-based mandates can only be welfare-improving if they operate through information correction or constraint relaxation, not intrinsic fairness

Key references

  • Kaplow & Shavell (2001) "Fairness versus Welfare" — Harvard Law Review
  • Kaplow & Shavell (2002) "Fairness versus Welfare" — Harvard University Press (book-length treatment)

Testable predictions

  • Court-mandated spending that ignores opportunity costs reduces aggregate welfare even holding total spending fixed
  • If courts enforce rights without redistribution (no tax adjustment), some agents must be worse off ex post
  • Empirical test: compare outcomes where courts enforce procedural rights (process-based) vs. substantive entitlements; only the former should survive a welfare comparison

Connection to our design

  • Net health effects (population-level) capture whether mandates are welfare-improving or dominated
  • If negative spillovers on non-litigants outweigh gains to litigants, the Kaplow-Shavell result is operative
  • Heterogeneity by claim type may reveal which mandates are instrumentally justified vs. purely rights-driven

2. Information and Allocation: Courts vs. Agencies

(a) Hayek (1945): Dispersed knowledge

  • Information about preferences, needs, and local conditions is dispersed and often tacit
  • Centralized allocation fails when information is local, rapidly changing, and costly to aggregate
  • Courts responding to individual cases may elicit otherwise unrevealed information (severity, urgency)
  • But: courts see idiosyncratic information; agencies see systemic trade-offs
  • Courts solve local information problems but create global inefficiencies

(b) Mechanism design: Myerson, Maskin

  • Efficient allocation requires mechanisms that elicit private information truthfully and respect incentive compatibility
  • Courts operate as ex post mechanisms: information revealed only after harm, no screening ex ante, weak incentive compatibility (agents exaggerate need)
  • Agencies design ex ante mechanisms (formularies, HTA, budgets)
  • Courts dominate when ex ante screening is impossible or unreliable
  • Agencies dominate when mechanisms can be designed to elicit information ex ante

(c) Weitzman (1974): Prices vs. quantities under uncertainty

  • Health budgets = quantity instruments; court orders = price-like mandates (must fund regardless of cost)
  • If marginal benefit uncertainty dominates → quantities preferred
  • If marginal cost uncertainty dominates → prices preferred
  • Judicial mandates more efficient when marginal benefit uncertainty is large (rare diseases, emergencies)
  • Inefficient when marginal costs explode (specialty drugs)

Key references

  • Hayek (1945) "The Use of Knowledge in Society" — AER
  • Myerson (1979) "Incentive Compatibility and the Bargaining Problem" — Econometrica
  • Maskin (1999) "Nash Equilibrium and Welfare Optimality" — Review of Economic Studies
  • Weitzman (1974) "Prices vs. Quantities" — Review of Economic Studies

Testable predictions

  • Courts outperform agencies when local/idiosyncratic information matters (heterogeneous conditions, emergencies)
  • Agencies outperform when standardized screening works (formulary drugs, routine procedures)
  • Court-mandated spending more efficient for rare/urgent claims, less efficient for common high-cost drugs
  • Grant rates near 100% regardless of claim type suggest courts are not effectively screening (weak IC)

Connection to our design

  • Heterogeneity by claim type maps to Hayek/Weitzman: rare diseases vs. common drugs
  • Judge variation in grant rates tests whether courts function as information-eliciting mechanisms or rubber stamps
  • Pre/post NATJUS comparison tests whether improving court information shifts toward agency-like efficiency

3. Health Capital and Diminishing Returns

Core model

  • Grossman (1972): health is a capital stock producing healthy time
  • Individuals invest until marginal cost = marginal benefit
  • Diminishing returns are fundamental to the model

Implication for court-mandated spending

  • Court mandates typically occur at high-cost margins — treatments for conditions already partially managed
  • Fund treatments late in the health production function where marginal returns are low
  • Reallocation from preventive/primary care reduces total health capital accumulation
  • Mandates welfare-improving only when they relax binding credit or insurance constraints at early margins

Key references

  • Grossman (1972) "On the Concept of Health Capital and the Demand for Health" — JPE

Testable predictions

  • Court-mandated treatments lie on the flat portion of the health production function
  • Marginal returns to judicial spending below inframarginal public health investments (FHP, vaccination, etc.)
  • Exception: mandates that provide access to cost-effective treatments denied by administrative failure should show high returns

Connection to our design

  • Judge IV estimates returns to judicially induced spending
  • Federal transfer IV benchmarks returns to administrative spending
  • Comparison identifies where on the production function each type of spending operates
  • Composition analysis: on-formulary (potentially high-return, supply failure) vs. off-formulary (potentially low-return, flat of the curve)

4. Optimal Earmarking

Core results

  • Buchanan (1963): earmarking restricts budget flexibility; equivalent to lump-sum transfers under full fungibility
  • Hines & Thaler (1995): empirically, grants "stick where they hit" (flypaper effect) — earmarks not fully offset
  • Brett & Keen (2000): earmarking can be optimal when voters face fiscal illusion
  • Dhillon & Perroni (2001): earmarking as commitment device when political commitment problems exist
  • Judicial mandates are de facto earmarks imposed ex post — particularly rigid because they specify items, patients, and procurement channels

Key references

  • Buchanan (1963) "The Economics of Earmarked Taxes" — JPE
  • Hines & Thaler (1995) "The Flypaper Effect" — JEP
  • Brett & Keen (2000) "Political Uncertainty and the Earmarking of Environmental Taxes" — JPublE
  • Dhillon & Perroni (2001) "Tax Earmarking and Grass-Roots Accountability" — Economics Letters

Testable predictions

  • If mandates are fungible: little effect on spending composition (municipalities offset elsewhere)
  • If flypaper: spending composition shifts toward litigated categories, away from discretionary
  • Earmarking welfare-improving only if courts target systematically underfunded but high-return health inputs
  • Earmarking welfare-reducing when it constrains adjustment to shocks (rigidity cost)

Connection to our design

  • Budget composition analysis: judge-IV effect on spending subcategories
  • Test fungibility: does judicial spending crowd out other health spending one-for-one, partially, or not at all?
  • Interaction with fiscal capacity: rigidity costs should be larger in budget-constrained municipalities
  • ICSAP (avoidable hospitalizations) as outcome captures whether crowd-out hits primary care

5. Regulatory Capture and Second-Best Courts

Core idea

  • Agencies may be captured by industry (Stigler 1971), distorted by career incentives (Laffont & Tirole 1991), or politically constrained (Shleifer & Vishny 1993)
  • In a second-best world, courts serve as an external enforcement device or constraint on captured regulators
  • Courts improve welfare when: agency bias is severe, capture is asymmetric, judicial decision-making is harder to capture
  • Courts reduce welfare when: litigation is itself captured, or judges lack technical capacity

Key references

  • Stigler (1971) "The Theory of Economic Regulation" — Bell Journal
  • Laffont & Tirole (1991) "The Politics of Government Decision-Making" — QJE
  • Shleifer & Vishny (1993) "Corruption" — QJE

Testable predictions

  • Judicial mandates welfare-improving when administrative allocation is distorted (political bias, underfunding, capture)
  • Welfare-reducing when litigation itself is captured (pharma-driven, repeat-player dominated)
  • Empirical test: interaction between litigation intensity and measures of agency capture/quality
  • Courts as corrective: effects should be larger where administrative capacity is weakest

Connection to our design

  • Heterogeneity by municipal administrative capacity, corruption indices, political alignment
  • If judicial spending has higher returns in poorly governed municipalities → courts as corrective
  • If returns are low everywhere or only in well-governed municipalities → courts not solving a capture problem

6. Property Rules vs. Liability Rules

Core framework

  • Calabresi & Melamed (1972): entitlements can be protected by property rules (injunctions) or liability rules (damages/compensation)
  • Court injunctions in health = property rules ("treatment must be provided")
  • Administrative discretion with compensation = liability rules (coverage decisions + appeals)

Key result

  • Property rules efficient when transaction costs are low and entitlement valuation is hard
  • Liability rules dominate when bargaining is costly and damages can be priced
  • Health context: valuation of health loss is genuinely difficult, but bargaining over budget allocation is also costly

Key references

  • Calabresi & Melamed (1972) "Property Rules, Liability Rules, and Inalienability" — Harvard Law Review

Testable predictions

  • Judicial mandates (property-rule enforcement) efficient only when valuation of health loss is infeasible and bargaining is impossible
  • Otherwise, liability-style compensation (administrative coverage with appeal rights) dominates
  • Prediction: injunctive orders for easily valued treatments (standard drugs with known prices) are inefficient; for hard-to-value conditions (rare diseases, emergencies) they may be justified

Connection to our design

  • Heterogeneity by claim type: standard vs. non-standard treatments
  • Whether property-rule enforcement (must provide) leads to worse outcomes than administrative processes with appeal mechanisms

7. Procurement and Moral Hazard

Core model

  • Laffont & Tirole (1993): efficient procurement requires competition, commitment, and price discipline
  • Court-ordered procurement bypasses auctions, eliminates commitment, weakens incentives for cost revelation
  • Suppliers face reduced incentive to price affordably or negotiate discounts when courts enforce coverage
  • Creates supplier moral hazard: pharma companies may encourage litigation as market access strategy

Key references

  • Laffont & Tirole (1993) "A Theory of Incentives in Procurement and Regulation" — MIT Press
  • Genicolo-Martins & Furquim de Azevedo (2024) — procurement cost premium (30-44% markup in urgent judicial tenders)

Testable predictions

  • Prices paid under court mandates exceed benchmark procurement prices
  • Suppliers invest less in cost reduction when judicial route available
  • Entry incentives distort toward litigation-friendly products (high-cost, patented, non-formulary)
  • Litigation concentrates on drugs not cost-effective enough to pass HTA (CONITEC) but profitable enough to litigate

Connection to our design

  • Composition of judicially mandated items (on-list vs. off-list, generic vs. branded)
  • Price comparisons: judicial vs. administrative procurement (Genicolo-Martins data)
  • Whether mandated treatments have evidence-based efficacy

8. Litigation, Selection, and Distribution

(a) Shavell (1982, 1997): Private vs. social incentives to sue

  • Private incentive to sue ≠ social incentive
  • Over-litigation when private gains exceed social benefits
  • Under-litigation for diffuse harms
  • In health: individuals capture full benefit of mandated treatment but impose diffuse costs on the system

(b) Bebchuk (1984): Litigation under asymmetric information

  • Stronger parties settle advantageously
  • Weaker parties drop claims
  • Predicts that better-resourced plaintiffs (or those with pharma/legal support) are more likely to litigate and win

(c) Priest & Klein (1984): Selection of disputes

  • Litigated cases are selected disputes, not representative
  • Close cases go to trial; clear-cut cases settle or are never filed
  • Implication: observed litigation may not represent the full distribution of unmet health needs

(d) Galanter (1974): Repeat players vs. one-shotters

  • Repeat players have lower unit costs, learning advantages, strategic behavior
  • One-shot litigants (typical patients) are disadvantaged
  • Exception: public defenders and patient associations can function as repeat players on behalf of the poor

Key references

  • Shavell (1982) "The Social versus the Private Incentive to Bring Suit" — JLS
  • Shavell (1997) "The Fundamental Divergence between the Private and the Social Motive to Use the Legal System" — JLS
  • Bebchuk (1984) "Litigation and Settlement under Imperfect Information" — RAND Journal
  • Priest & Klein (1984) "The Selection of Disputes for Litigation" — JLS
  • Galanter (1974) "Why the 'Haves' Come Out Ahead" — Law & Society Review

Testable predictions

  • Litigation skews toward higher-income, better-informed, repeat or supported plaintiffs
  • Progressive litigation requires: low filing costs, legal aid, collective/class mechanisms, standardized claims
  • Otherwise, redistribution is regressive in equilibrium
  • Public defenders as repeat players: where Defensoria Pública is strong, litigation composition should be more progressive

Connection to our design

  • Heterogeneity by municipality income/HDI and Defensoria presence
  • Plaintiff characteristics (where available): income, representation type
  • Whether judicial spending narrows or widens health outcome gaps across municipalities
  • Test Galanter prediction: effects of litigation may differ by legal representation type

9. Fiscal Federalism and Unfunded Mandates

Core idea

  • Oates (1972, 1999): fiscal decentralization assigns spending to the level of government with best information, but assumes appropriate budget constraints and matching of responsibilities to revenues
  • Judicial mandates violate this assignment: courts interpreting federal constitutional rights impose costs on municipal budgets without corresponding revenue transfers
  • Solidary liability (Art. 23) means any level of government can be ordered to pay; in practice municipalities bear costs that SUS rules assign to states or federal government
  • Creates a vertical fiscal externality: court orders at the state level generate spending obligations at the municipal level

Soft budget constraint connection

  • Kornai (1986), Kornai, Maskin & Roland (2003): when agents expect bailouts, budget discipline erodes
  • In the judicial context, the reverse problem: courts impose hard mandates (immediate compliance, bank account seizure) on municipal budgets designed with certain flexibility
  • But also a forward-looking SBC: if municipalities expect courts to mandate spending, they may under-invest in administrative capacity for drug delivery (why build procurement systems if courts will order emergency purchases anyway?)

Key references

  • Oates (1972) "Fiscal Federalism" — Harcourt Brace
  • Oates (1999) "An Essay on Fiscal Federalism" — JEL, 37(3)
  • Kornai (1986) "The Soft Budget Constraint" — Kyklos, 39(1)
  • Kornai, Maskin & Roland (2003) "Understanding the Soft Budget Constraint" — JEL, 41(4)

Testable predictions

  • Judicial spending has larger negative effects in municipalities that bear costs misassigned from higher levels of government (vertical mismatch)
  • Municipalities with less fiscal capacity (lower own-source revenue) are more vulnerable to mandate shocks
  • If SBC logic operates: municipalities with more litigation history may invest less in administrative pharmaceutical delivery
  • Post-Tema 793 (2019, rationalizing cost assignment) and post-2024 federal reimbursement mechanism: effects should attenuate if vertical mismatch is resolved

Connection to our design

  • Paper documents that 55% of SP municipal judicial pharma spending was for state/federal-responsibility drugs (Wang 2014)
  • Heterogeneity by fiscal capacity and revenue composition
  • Pre/post Tema 793 and 2024 reimbursement reforms as natural breaks
  • Whether crowd-out is driven by mandates within municipal responsibility vs. misassigned mandates

10. Supplier-Induced Demand

Core idea

  • Arrow (1963): health care markets fail because of information asymmetry between providers and patients; providers can exploit this to shift demand
  • Evans (1974), McGuire (2000): physician-induced demand (PID) — doctors can recommend treatments beyond what a fully informed patient would choose, because patients cannot evaluate clinical necessity independently
  • In the judicial context: the doctor-lawyer-pharma referral chain creates a demand inducement channel via the legal system
  • Physicians prescribe specific (often branded, non-formulary) drugs → refer patients to specialized law firms → courts order provision at public expense
  • This is PID mediated through litigation rather than direct provision

Key references

  • Arrow (1963) "Uncertainty and the Welfare Economics of Medical Care" — AER, 53(5)
  • Evans (1974) "Supplier-Induced Demand: Some Empirical Evidence and Implications" — in Perlman (ed.), The Economics of Health and Medical Care
  • McGuire (2000) "Physician Agency" — Handbook of Health Economics, Vol. 1, Ch. 9
  • Dranove (1988) "Demand Inducement and the Physician/Patient Relationship" — Economic Inquiry, 26(2)

Testable predictions

  • Supply-driven litigation concentrates on high-margin, non-formulary, patented drugs
  • Lawyer concentration predicts litigation volume independent of health need (19 lawyers filed 63% of SP cases)
  • Physician prescribing patterns cluster around specific drugs associated with pharma marketing
  • If SID dominates: health returns to supply-driven cases should be lower than demand-driven cases (patients with genuine unmet needs)
  • Reducing supply-side incentives (e.g., pharma enforcement operations) should reduce litigation without worsening health outcomes

Connection to our design

  • Paper's supply-side section (Sec 7.4) documents lawyer concentration and pharma networks
  • Split by high-volume vs. low-volume lawyers tests whether supply-driven margin has different health returns
  • Composition of mandated items (branded vs. generic, on-list vs. off-list) as proxy for SID
  • Operação Garra Rufa / eculizumab as case studies of extreme SID

11. Dynamic Commitment and Ratchet Effects

Core idea

  • Freixas, Guesnerie & Tirole (1985): under incomplete information, past performance constrains future flexibility (ratchet effect)
  • Applied to judicial mandates: once a court orders a chronic medication, the commitment is indefinite — mandates accumulate rather than expire
  • Each successful lawsuit creates a recurring budget obligation; the stock of active mandates grows over time even if new filing rates are constant
  • Budget rigidification: the share of the health budget pre-committed to judicial mandates ratchets upward, shrinking the discretionary margin

Precedent ratchet

  • Judicial precedent creates a one-way ratchet: once a treatment is established as judicially obtainable, denial becomes harder
  • Lawyers cite prior grants; judges face reputational/legal costs of diverging from established patterns
  • Combined with supply-side inducement, this creates a self-reinforcing cycle: precedent → more cases → more precedent

Key references

  • Freixas, Guesnerie & Tirole (1985) "Planning under Incomplete Information and the Ratchet Effect" — Review of Economic Studies, 52(2)
  • Kornai, Maskin & Roland (2003) "Understanding the Soft Budget Constraint" — JEL, 41(4)

Testable predictions

  • Cumulative stock of active mandates (not just flow) predicts budget pressure and health outcomes
  • Municipalities with longer litigation histories have less fiscal flexibility and worse health indicators, controlling for current spending
  • Dynamic event-study: effects worsen over time as mandates accumulate (not just contemporaneous shock)
  • New mandate effects larger when existing stock of mandates is high (nonlinear/convex costs of rigidity)

Connection to our design

  • Paper's event-study specification (Sec 5.3) can capture dynamic accumulation
  • Stock vs. flow distinction in the first stage: current-year judge assignments vs. accumulated mandates
  • Whether crowd-out effects are larger in municipalities with longer litigation exposure

12. Behavioral Judicial Decision-Making

Core idea

  • Judges are subject to systematic cognitive biases that affect the quality of health resource allocation via courts
  • Standard economic models assume judges optimize (or at least act as rational agents); behavioral evidence suggests systematic departures

(a) Identifiable victim effect

  • Schelling (1968): people allocate more resources to save identified individuals than statistical lives
  • Judges see the specific patient in the courtroom; the statistical lives lost from crowd-out of primary care are invisible
  • Predicts systematic over-granting: the identifiable patient always "wins" against the abstract population

(b) Omission bias and action bias

  • Denying a treatment feels like causing harm (action); granting feels like helping (but the crowd-out harm is omission)
  • Judges face asymmetric reputational risk: denial may lead to patient death attributed to the judge; granting spreads costs diffusely across the budget
  • Consistent with 93% grant rates regardless of clinical merit

(c) Anchoring on plaintiff evidence

  • Guthrie, Rachlinski & Wistrich (2001): judges anchor on initial information presented
  • In health cases, plaintiff presents medical prescription and clinical urgency; defendant (municipality) presents budget constraints and formulary rules
  • Anchoring on clinical framing shifts decisions toward granting

(d) Sequential decision patterns

  • Chen, Moskowitz & Shue (2016): decision-makers exhibit gambler's fallacy in sequential decisions — after a streak of similar decisions, they switch
  • May generate random-looking variation in grant rates that is actually driven by sequence position rather than case merits

Key references

  • Schelling (1968) "The Life You Save May Be Your Own" — in Chase (ed.), Problems in Public Expenditure Analysis
  • Guthrie, Rachlinski & Wistrich (2001) "Inside the Judicial Mind" — Cornell Law Review, 86
  • Jolls, Sunstein & Thaler (1998) "A Behavioral Approach to Law and Economics" — Stanford Law Review, 50
  • Chen, Moskowitz & Shue (2016) "Decision Making Under the Gambler's Fallacy" — QJE, 131(3)

Testable predictions

  • Grant rates near 100% despite heterogeneous clinical merit → identifiable victim effect + omission bias dominating
  • Judge variation driven by cognitive style (some judges more susceptible to anchoring) rather than policy preferences
  • Sequential patterns in grant/deny decisions within judge (Chen et al.)
  • NATJUS technical notes reduce anchoring on plaintiff evidence: post-NATJUS grant rates should be more sensitive to clinical merit
  • Judges who consult NATJUS should show more variation in grant rates (discriminating between cases) vs. rubber-stamping judges

Connection to our design

  • Judge heterogeneity in grant rates may reflect cognitive differences, not just policy preferences
  • Whether NATJUS consultation predicts better allocation (higher health returns per mandate)
  • Can test Chen et al. sequential patterns using case-level data within judges
  • If behavioral biases dominate: reforms improving judicial information (NATJUS) should matter more than reforms tightening legal standards (Tema 106)

Overarching Framing for the Paper

Theoretical benchmark

  • Judicial mandates for health spending are never first-best in standard economic theory
  • They can be second-best corrections to capture, information failure, or political neglect
  • They are welfare-reducing when they function as absolute rights, rigid earmarks, or procurement bypasses

Central empirical question (theory-derived)

When do courts operate as corrective mechanisms, and when do they create predictable distortions?

Two polar views

Optimistic (courts as corrective):

  • Administrative allocation fails due to capture, underfunding, or political neglect (Stigler, Laffont-Tirole)
  • Courts correct under-provision by eliciting local information (Hayek) and enforcing entitlements
  • Earmarking corrects political underprovision (Dhillon & Perroni 2001, Brett & Keen 2000)
  • Courts force higher governments to meet their fiscal obligations (correcting vertical mismatch, Oates)
  • Predicts: positive health returns, progressive incidence, effects concentrated where admin capacity is weakest

Pessimistic (courts as distortionary):

  • Courts lack information for efficient allocation (mechanism design); mandates at flat of production function (Grossman)
  • Rights-based rules Pareto-dominated (Kaplow-Shavell)
  • Litigation captured by repeat players and pharma (Galanter, Laffont-Tirole procurement)
  • Supplier-induced demand inflates litigation beyond clinical need (Arrow, McGuire, Evans)
  • Rigid earmarks reduce allocative flexibility; crowd out high-return spending
  • Ratchet effects: mandates accumulate, progressively rigidifying budgets (Freixas-Guesnerie-Tirole)
  • Vertical fiscal externalities: costs fall on municipalities that lack capacity to absorb them (Oates, Kornai)
  • Behavioral biases (identifiable victim, omission bias) produce systematic over-granting (Schelling, Guthrie et al.)
  • Predicts: low/negative health returns, regressive incidence, crowd-out of PHC, escalating fiscal burden

Our contribution

  • Causal estimates that distinguish between these views
  • Judge-IV design identifies whether judicially induced spending improves or worsens population health
  • Heterogeneity analysis maps to specific theoretical mechanisms (claim type, municipal capacity, plaintiff characteristics, legal representation, pre/post reform)
  • Comparison with administrative spending returns (federal transfer IV) directly tests relative allocative efficiency
  • Supply-side analysis (lawyer concentration splits) tests demand inducement channel
  • Dynamic event-study captures ratchet/accumulation effects beyond contemporaneous shocks

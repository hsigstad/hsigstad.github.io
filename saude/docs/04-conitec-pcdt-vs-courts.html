CONITEC vs Courts

CONITEC/PCDT vs Court Orders: Recurring Points of Conflict

Overview

This memo analyzes the systematic tensions between health technology assessment (CONITEC decisions, PCDT protocols) and judicial orders. Understanding these conflicts is essential for predicting litigation patterns and outcomes.

1. The Institutional Framework

CONITEC (Comissão Nacional de Incorporação de Tecnologias no SUS)

Role: Evaluates whether medications, procedures, and health products should be incorporated into SUS.

Process:

  1. Submission (manufacturer, society, ministry)
  2. Technical analysis (efficacy, safety, cost-effectiveness)
  3. Public consultation
  4. Plenary deliberation
  5. Ministry of Health decision
  6. If positive → incorporation → RENAME/PCDT update

Criteria:

  • Clinical efficacy (evidence-based)
  • Safety profile
  • Cost-effectiveness (ICER thresholds)
  • Budget impact
  • Equity considerations

PCDTs (Protocolos Clínicos e Diretrizes Terapêuticas)

Role: Define standard treatment pathways for conditions covered by SUS.

Content:

  • Diagnostic criteria
  • Treatment lines (first-line, second-line, etc.)
  • Dosing and duration
  • Monitoring requirements
  • Exclusion criteria

2. Core Conflicts

2.1 The Evidence Gap

CONITEC perspective:

"The evidence does not demonstrate superiority over existing treatments to justify the additional cost."

Judicial perspective:

"The treating physician prescribed it; the prescription demonstrates medical necessity."

Conflict: Courts often accept individual prescriptions as sufficient evidence, while CONITEC requires population-level evidence of comparative effectiveness.

2.2 Cost-Effectiveness Threshold

CONITEC applies thresholds:

  • Implicit ICER threshold: ~1-3x GDP per capita per QALY
  • Budget impact analysis for affordability

Courts generally don't consider:

  • Cost-effectiveness ratios
  • Opportunity costs (what won't be funded if this is)
  • System-wide budget impacts

Typical judicial reasoning:

"O direito à saúde não pode ser condicionado a critérios econômicos quando a vida está em risco."

2.3 Timeline Mismatch

CONITEC Process Judicial Process
180 days (standard) Days to weeks (tutela urgência)
Up to 360 days (complex) Immediate if emergency

Conflict: Patients litigate while CONITEC is still evaluating, or before CONITEC even receives submission.

2.4 Individual vs Population

CONITEC: Makes population-level decisions

  • "On average, this drug doesn't improve outcomes enough"
  • "For most patients, the existing treatment is adequate"

Courts: Decide individual cases

  • "THIS patient's doctor says they need THIS drug"
  • "THIS patient didn't respond to standard treatment"

3. The "CONITEC Rejected" Problem

When CONITEC Says "No"

Three scenarios:

  1. Not submitted: CONITEC never evaluated

    • Courts more willing to grant (Tema 6 may apply)

  2. Rejected: CONITEC evaluated and recommended against

    • Post-Tema 6: Strong deference expected
    • But courts sometimes still grant if "individual circumstances"

  3. Incorporated with restrictions: CONITEC said yes, but only for specific indications

    • Courts may expand to other indications (off-label)

Judicial Responses to CONITEC Rejection

Deferential response:

"A CONITEC, órgão técnico competente, avaliou a tecnologia e não recomendou sua incorporação. O Judiciário não pode substituir-se ao administrador na definição de políticas públicas de saúde."

Non-deferential response:

"A decisão da CONITEC não vincula o Judiciário quando demonstrada, no caso concreto, a necessidade individual do tratamento e a ineficácia das alternativas terapêuticas."

4. PCDT Conflicts in Practice

4.1 Line of Treatment Disputes

PCDT says:

"First-line: Drug A. Second-line (if A fails): Drug B. Third-line: Drug C."

Patient wants:

"Drug C immediately, without trying A and B."

Court response (typical):

If physician documents why A and B aren't appropriate for this patient, court may grant C directly.

4.2 Diagnostic Criteria Disputes

PCDT says:

"Eligible patients must have [specific lab values, symptoms, etc.]"

Patient presents:

Borderline criteria, or criteria met by different methodology

Conflict: Is the patient "in" or "out" of PCDT coverage?

4.3 Duration and Dosing

PCDT says:

"Maximum duration: 12 months" or "Maximum dose: X mg"

Physician prescribes:

Continuation beyond 12 months, or higher dose

Courts: Often grant if physician justifies clinical need.

5. The "Therapeutic Substitute" Battlefield

Key Tema 6 Requirement

Plaintiff must show "inexistência de substituto terapêutico incorporado pelo SUS"

What Counts as a "Substitute"?

Strict view:

Any drug in the same therapeutic class that treats the condition.

Narrow view:

Only a drug with equivalent efficacy and tolerability for THIS patient.

Courts increasingly adopt narrow view:

"Não basta a existência de medicamento da mesma classe terapêutica; é necessário que seja efetivamente equivalente para o paciente."

Common Disputes

Requested Drug SUS Offers Court Often Says
Brand-name Generic Generic is equivalent → Deny
Newer biologic Older biologic May grant if documented failure on older
Oral formulation Injectable May grant for compliance/convenience
Combination drug Separate drugs Usually deny (same effect)

6. The "Evidence-Based Medicine" Standard

Tema 6 Requirement

"Comprovação de eficácia do medicamento à luz da medicina baseada em evidências"

What Evidence Courts Accept

Strong evidence (usually sufficient):

  • ANVISA registration for the indication
  • Randomized controlled trials
  • Meta-analyses/systematic reviews
  • International guideline recommendations

Weaker evidence (contested):

  • Case series
  • Expert opinion letters
  • Off-label use without trials
  • Foreign approvals without Brazilian registration

The "ANVISA Registered" Shortcut

Common judicial reasoning:

"O medicamento possui registro na ANVISA, o que atesta sua segurança e eficácia."

Problem: ANVISA registration ≠ CONITEC incorporation

  • ANVISA: Is it safe and effective? (Yes/No)
  • CONITEC: Is it worth the cost vs. alternatives? (Comparative)

7. Recurring Case Types

7.1 Oncology

CONITEC challenge:

  • New drugs are expensive (R$20-50k/month)
  • Evidence often from single-arm trials
  • Multiple indications requested rapidly

Court tendency:

  • High grant rate for cancer drugs
  • "Risco de vida" argument is compelling
  • Less scrutiny of cost-effectiveness

Typical drugs: Pembrolizumabe, Nivolumabe, targeted therapies

7.2 Rare Diseases

CONITEC challenge:

  • Small patient populations
  • Limited evidence (rare disease = rare data)
  • Extremely high costs (R$50-150k/month)

Court tendency:

  • High grant rate
  • "No alternative" argument works well
  • Less emphasis on evidence quality

Typical drugs: Enzyme replacement therapies, gene therapies

7.3 Autoimmune/Inflammatory

CONITEC position:

  • Many biologics incorporated
  • Specific indications defined in PCDTs
  • Step therapy required

Court tendency:

  • Grant when step therapy documented as failed
  • Grant for off-label indications with evidence
  • Moderate scrutiny

Typical drugs: Adalimumabe, Infliximabe, Secuquinumabe

7.4 Mental Health

CONITEC gap:

  • Fewer mental health drugs evaluated
  • PCDTs less comprehensive

Court tendency:

  • Variable outcomes
  • Increasing litigation for newer antipsychotics, ketamine, cannabis

8. Post-2024 Changes (SVs 60-61)

Expected Impact on Conflicts

Before:

  • Courts freely substituted CONITEC judgment
  • Administrative path often bypassed
  • NAT-Jus consultation optional

After:

  • Stronger deference to CONITEC expected
  • Administrative exhaustion required (SV 60)
  • NAT-Jus consultation institutionalized
  • Interfederative agreements structure process

Remaining Gaps

Even post-2024, conflicts persist when:

  • CONITEC hasn't evaluated the drug yet
  • Patient is "different" from PCDT population
  • New evidence emerges after CONITEC decision
  • Off-label use for rare situations

9. Strategic Implications

For Plaintiffs (Patients)

Stronger cases:

  • CONITEC hasn't evaluated (not rejected)
  • Documented failure on PCDT alternatives
  • Life-threatening condition
  • Strong evidence (trials, guidelines)

Weaker cases:

  • CONITEC explicitly rejected
  • Haven't tried PCDT alternatives
  • Convenience/preference-based
  • Weak evidence (anecdotes, case reports)

For State Defense

Stronger defenses:

  • CONITEC evaluated and rejected with reasons
  • Effective substitute available in PCDT
  • Plaintiff hasn't tried standard treatment
  • Evidence doesn't support superiority

Weaker defenses:

  • "Budget constraints" alone
  • "Not incorporated" without explaining why
  • PCDT exists but patient clearly outside criteria

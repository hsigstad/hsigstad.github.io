1. The Brazilian Public Health System (SUS)

<!-- Updated: 2026-02-09 | Sources: CF/88, Lei 8.080/1990, Portaria GM/MS 1.555/2013, LC 141/2012, castro2019lancet, macinko2006jech, cnm2022, cgu2021conitec, cfm2024, folha2025incorporation -->

Constitutional framework

  • Health is a right of all and a duty of the State. [CF/88 Art. 196]
  • Health services form a regionalized, hierarchical network constituting the Unified Health System (SUS). Three directives: decentralization, comprehensive care (integralidade), community participation. [CF/88 Art. 198]
  • All three levels of government (Union, States, Municipalities) share responsibility for health (competência comum). [CF/88 Art. 23(II)]

Organization

SUS is organized by the Lei Orgânica da Saúde. Municipalities are the primary executors of health policy (decentralization principle). [Lei 8.080/1990, Arts. 6–7] The Comissão Intergestores Tripartite (CIT) coordinates between federal, state, and municipal health managers. [Lei 8.080/1990 Art. 14-A; CIT Resolução Consolidada 2021]

Pharmaceutical assistance components

Medications within SUS are organized into three financing components, each defining which level of government is responsible for procurement and cost:

Component Responsibility Coverage Source
CBAF (Componente Básico) Municipality finances and acquires Basic drugs for primary care (diabetes, hypertension) [Portaria GM/MS 1.555/2013]
CESAF (Componente Estratégico) Union acquires; States distribute Drugs for endemic/strategic diseases (HIV, TB, malaria) [Portaria GM/MS 1.555/2013]
CEAF (Componente Especializado) Shared by tiers (see below) High-cost drugs for chronic/rare diseases [Portaria GM/MS 1.555/2013]

CEAF is further divided [Portaria GM/MS 1.555/2013]:

  • Group 1A: Union finances and acquires.
  • Group 1B: Union finances; States acquire.
  • Group 2: States finance and acquire.
  • Group 3: Municipal responsibility.

RENAME and CONITEC

  • RENAME (Relação Nacional de Medicamentos Essenciais): the national list of drugs that SUS is obligated to provide. Updated periodically. [Lei 8.080/1990 Art. 19-M, as amended by Lei 12.401/2011]
  • CONITEC (Comissão Nacional de Incorporação de Tecnologias no SUS): evaluates drugs for incorporation into SUS based on efficacy, safety, and cost-effectiveness. A drug not on RENAME is "not incorporated" and not routinely provided by SUS. [Lei 12.401/2011]

Population coverage

  • Approximately 75% of the population (~160 million people) relies exclusively on SUS for health care. The remaining ~25% hold supplementary private insurance, concentrated among higher-income households, but retain the right to use SUS services. [castro2019lancet]

Service delivery tiers

  • Primary care: Family Health Strategy (ESF) covers over 60% of the population. [macinko2006jech] NEEDS PRIMARY SOURCE — 2006 figure may be outdated; current ESF coverage likely higher
  • Secondary/tertiary care: Public hospitals, contracted private providers, and university hospitals organized into regional referral networks.

Fiscal decentralization

  • Municipal share of total public health spending rose from 29% (2012) to 34% (2022); federal share declined from 45% to 38%. NEEDS PRIMARY SOURCE — cited without attribution in the paper; likely SIOPS or CONASEMS data.
  • Municipalities bear increasing financial responsibility for health while also being the most exposed to court-mandated spending.

Service delivery gaps

  • Over 80% of municipalities reported shortages of basic medicines in their public pharmacies (2022 national survey). [cnm2022]
  • Federal audit: none of 25 sampled medications met the statutory 180-day availability deadline after CONITEC incorporation. [cgu2021conitec]
  • Estimated 900,000 patients awaiting elective procedures nationally. [cfm2024]
  • Nearly one-third of medicines incorporated since 2018 remained unavailable to patients, with an average delay of 648 days from incorporation to delivery (as of early 2025). [folha2025incorporation]

Health spending floors

The Constitution mandates minimum health spending [EC 29/2000; LC 141/2012]:

  • Municipalities: 15% of own-source revenue.
  • States: 12% of own-source revenue.
  • Union: formula-based floor linked to prior-year spending + GDP growth.

Failure to meet floors or to report to SIOPS can result in suspension of federal/state transfers. [LC 141/2012 Art. 26]

2. Court System Structure

<!-- Updated: 2026-02-06 | Sources: TJSP data, CNJ Res. 238/2016, CF/88 | 2 items unverified (vara counts) -->

TJSP (Tribunal de Justiça de São Paulo)

TJSP is the state court for São Paulo — the largest court system in Latin America by caseload. [CNJ, Justiça em Números]

Structure:

  • 322 comarcas handle health litigation. [TJSP full collapsed sentença data]
  • 3,006 unique judges active in health cases across these comarcas. [TJSP full collapsed sentença data]
  • São Paulo Capital has multiple Varas da Fazenda Pública (exact count not confirmed in available sources — verify via TJSP website).
  • Interior comarcas typically have 1–3 varas da fazenda pública each (verify via TJSP organizational data).

Varas da Fazenda Pública are the specialized courts that hear cases against the government (municipal and state). Health cases against public entities are filed here. [CPC Art. 64; NSCGJ/TJSP]

Health specialization: In comarcas with more than one Vara da Fazenda Pública, the court must specialize one vara for public health matters, with compensating redistribution of other case types. [CNJ Resolution 238/2016 Art. 3]

CADIP (Centro de Apoio ao Direito Público) provides guidance to judges via the "Caderno de Judicialização da Saúde." [TJSP/CADIP]

TRF3 (Tribunal Regional Federal da 3ª Região)

TRF3 is the federal court covering São Paulo and Mato Grosso do Sul. [CF/88 Art. 106]

  • Handles cases against the Union and INSS. [CF/88 Art. 109]
  • Also has automated random case assignment. [CPC Art. 284–285; CNJ administrative rules]
  • CNJ Resolution 238/2016 also applies (health specialization). [CNJ Res. 238/2016]
  • Much smaller volume than TJSP for health cases. Handles cases against the Union, which include high-cost drugs under CEAF Group 1A. [CF/88 Art. 109; Portaria GM/MS 1.555/2013]

Jurisdiction rules

Scenario Court Defendant Source
Drug on RENAME (CBAF) TJSP Municipality [SUS administrative rules]
Drug on RENAME (CEAF Groups 1–2) TJSP or TRF3 State or Union [SUS administrative rules]
Drug not on RENAME, cost <210 min. salaries TJSP State or Municipality [STF Tema 1.234 (2024)]
Drug not on RENAME, cost ≥210 min. salaries TRF3 Union [STF Tema 1.234 (2024)]
Drug without ANVISA registration TRF3 Union [STF Tema 500 (2019)]

3. Case Assignment (Distribuição)

<!-- Updated: 2026-02-06 | Sources: CPC, NSCGJ, CNJ Res. 238/2016 | Fully sourced -->

Random assignment mechanism

Case assignment at both TJSP and TRF3 is conducted through an automated, sequential distribution system that is effectively random. The system ensures balanced workloads and prevents litigants from selecting a specific judge. [CPC Art. 284–285; NSCGJ/TJSP]

Exceptions to random assignment

  • Prevenção (prevention): If a related case exists (same causa de pedir or same object), the new case is routed to the judge who first presided. [CPC Art. 59]
  • Conexão and continência: Connected or contained cases may be joined before the same judge for judicial economy. [CPC Arts. 55–57]
  • Health specialization (CNJ 238/2016): In comarcas with multiple varas da fazenda pública, health cases go to the specialized vara. This is random conditional on filing in a health-specialized vara. [CNJ Res. 238/2016]

Implications for Judge IV design

  • Random assignment within a vara/comarca is confirmed. Supports the core identification assumption. [CPC; NSCGJ]
  • Prevenção is the main threat: related cases routed to the same judge create non-random clustering. Need to identify and handle these. [CPC Art. 59]
  • Specialization reduces the judge pool in some comarcas but makes assignment cleaner. [CNJ Res. 238/2016]
  • No judge-shopping by litigants — automated system prevents this. [CPC Art. 284–285; NSCGJ/TJSP]

4. Health Litigation Process

<!-- Updated: 2026-02-09 | Sources: CNJ/Insper 2019, TJSP data, Ipea 2025, STJ Temas 84/98, TCU, tcu2017, biehl2009lancet, chieffi2009csp, ipea_anadep2013, biehl2015csp -->

Filing

  • Plaintiff (patient) files in their home comarca (domicile). [CPC Art. 46]
  • For suits against municipalities: filed in the comarca where the municipality is located (usually the same as plaintiff's domicile). [CPC Art. 46; TJSP Súmula 37]
  • For suits against the State: plaintiff can choose the state capital or their own domicile. [CPC Art. 52]
  • Private attorneys represent the vast majority of litigants, especially in health plan cases (~55% of TJSP annual health volume). [Sintese Tjsp Planos Vs Sus, p. 811; CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 107]
  • Defensoria Pública handles approximately 6.91% of first-instance sentencing volume at TJSP. Eligibility: family income up to 3 minimum salaries. Coverage is concentrated in urban hubs; interior comarcas have significant gaps. [TJSP Sentencas Summary, p. 1364; CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 107]
  • Defensoria coverage gap: public defenders were absent from 72% of Brazilian comarcas as of 2013. [ipea_anadep2013]
  • Defensoria litigation effect: Defensoria presence is associated with a seven-fold increase in the probability of health litigation. [biehl2015csp]
  • Ministério Público handles 3.11% of first-instance cases, primarily collective actions (Ações Civis Públicas). [TJSP Sentencas Summary, p. 1364]
  • Only ~20% of health litigants are identified as economically vulnerable. [CNJ 2021, p. 269]
  • Litigation concentration: 6 lawyers were responsible for 35% of all health cases against the state in a single year; a single physician wrote 66% of prescriptions for a specific judicialized drug. [TCU Orcamento Judicializacao, p. 980]

Case composition

  • Medicines dominate: approximately 80% of health cases. [tcu2017]
  • On-formulary share: over 50% of medication lawsuits in some jurisdictions request drugs already on SUS formularies, suggesting litigation often addresses administrative delivery failures rather than genuine coverage gaps. [biehl2009lancet; chieffi2009csp]
  • Beyond medicines: lawsuits seek surgical procedures, hospital beds, and medical devices.

Preliminary injunctions (tutela antecipada)

  • Judges frequently grant preliminary injunctions in health cases, with a median of 10 days to grant at TJSP. [Tutela Provisoria Senado, p. 1443–1444]
  • There is a documented "automatism" in granting: judges often defer to the medical prescription without consulting health managers or technical bodies. [CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 84]
  • Since 2016, judges are encouraged to consult NAT-Jus via the e-NatJus system for technical opinions. However, fewer than 6% of decisions cite technical standards like CONITEC or RENAME (national first-instance average). [CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 91]
  • TJSP specifically: fewer than 0.01% of first-instance health decisions cited NAT-Jus — 9 of 107,497 cases. [CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 91]
  • Geographic variation is substantial: e.g., Mato Grosso courts cited technical notes in 18% of cases. [CNJ/Insper 2019]
  • Average time from filing to full sentence at TJSP: approximately one year and nine months. [Tutela Provisoria Senado, p. 1444]
  • National average from filing to final judgment (trânsito em julgado): 432 days. [Stf Temas Desafios Academico, p. 889]

Grant rates

Scope Grant rate Source
All health cases (TJSP, incl. disability/plans) ~67% [TJSP sentença data]
SUS cases against municipalities (full + partial) ~87% [TJSP sentença data]
Medication/treatment cases against municipalities ~93% [TJSP sentença data]
Preliminary injunctions (SP city) ~85% [Wang & Ferraz 2013]
Brazilian health litigation (general) >90% [Biehl et al. 2012]

Approximately 10.4% of TJSP sentences are partially granted (limiting duration, requiring periodic reports, allowing generic substitution). [TJSP sentença data]

Margins of judicial discretion

Even with high grant rates, judges vary on several margins [CNJ/Insper 2019; TJSP Sentencas Summary]:

  1. Speed: Immediate injunction (days) vs. waiting for full trial (months/years).
  2. Scope: Exact brand-name drug vs. generic substitute vs. SUS alternative. Enunciado 28 (CNJ Health Journeys) directs judges to require technical descriptions rather than brand names. [Enunciado 28]
  3. Enforcement intensity: Direct fund seizure (bloqueio/sequestro) from municipal bank accounts vs. softer compliance mechanisms.
  4. Partial vs. full grant: Limiting duration, requiring periodic medical reports (Enunciado 2), or capping quantities.
  5. NAT-Jus consultation: Whether the judge seeks and follows technical advice.
  6. Financial incapacity verification: Under Tema 6 (STF), judges must verify plaintiff cannot afford the treatment (for non-incorporated drugs).

Enforcement and compliance

When a government entity fails to comply with a court order, three enforcement tools are available:

  1. Bloqueio/sequestro de verbas (fund seizure): Direct seizure of funds from the municipal bank account. Most common and effective tool. Legality established under STJ Tema 84. [Tjsp Caderno Judicializacao 2024, p. 1038]
  2. Multa diária (astreintes): Daily fines on the public entity to compel compliance. Validated by STJ Tema 98. [Tjsp Caderno Judicializacao 2024, p. 1038]
  3. Responsabilização pessoal: Public officials (including health secretaries) can face personal liability for non-compliance, including charges for crime of disobedience under Penal Code Art. 330. [Mandado Seguranca Lei 12016, p. 630]

Fund seizures are described by managers as "catastrophic" for fiscal planning, as they extract liquidity regardless of existing municipal commitments. [CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 202]

Only 50–60% of municipal health secretariats have dedicated legal or management units to handle judicial demands. [CNJ 2021, p. 270]

Appeals

  • Approximately 55% of first-instance health cases are appealed to the second instance (national data, 2008–2017: 277,411 of 498,715 cases). [CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 87]
  • States and municipalities are the primary appellants, often appealing as an administrative duty regardless of success probability. [CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 88]
  • Reversals are rare in practice because most orders are already enforced via preliminary injunction, and many cases are rendered moot by compliance. [CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 84]
  • Treatments are treated as "irrepetíveis" (non-returnable) once consumed, especially for financially incapacitated patients. The fiscal impact occurs at the preliminary stage and persists regardless of appellate outcome. [Tutela Provisoria Mpsp, p. 1411–1412; Stf Tema 6, p. 885]
  • The Câmaras de Direito Público at TJSP handle SUS appeals and apply binding precedents (e.g., Tema 793). They increasingly consult NAT-Jus before confirming/reversing lower court decisions. [Sintese Tjsp Planos Vs Sus, p. 820; CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 91; Sumulas Vinculantes 60 61 Saude, p. 916]

Procurement of judicially mandated medicines

Municipalities rely on emergency purchases (dispensa de licitação) because court orders mandate compliance within 24 hours to 15 days. Standard bidding under Law 8.666/1993 is too slow. [Ipea 2025, p. 557; Conass Financiamento Sus 2023, p. 383; CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 334]

Judges sometimes grant direct access to municipal accounts, allowing the plaintiff to purchase at a commercial pharmacy at market prices. [CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 200]

Price benchmarks: STF Tema 1.234 (2024) set a ceiling at the PMVG at zero-percent tax or the CONITEC incorporation discount, whichever is lower. [Stf Temas 6 1234 Manual Pge, p. 878] However, pre-2024 no effective price constraint existed.

The 30–44% cost premium (vs. standard SUS procurement) is explained by:

  • Retail buying: single-unit purchases at higher unit costs. [CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 196; Ipea 2025, p. 577]
  • Brand specificity: courts mandate specific brands rather than generics. [Enunciados Jornadas Saude Consolidado, p. 405; Ipea 2025, p. 577]
  • Strategic industry behavior: pharmaceutical companies delay ANVISA registration or CONITEC incorporation to profit from higher non-negotiated prices in the judicial channel. [Ipea 2025, p. 577]

Administrative paths: 52.1% of municipalities have created extrajudicial provision mechanisms to prevent lawsuits. [Ipea 2025, p. 545, 578] Some regions use inter-municipal consortiums for pooled procurement. [CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 303]

5. Defendant Identity and Fiscal Incidence

<!-- Updated: 2026-02-09 | Sources: CF/88, STF Temas 793/1.234, Fiocruz 2020, Ipea 2025, wang2014rap, folha2025elevidys, theconversation2024, diniz2014csc -->

Solidary liability

Under the Constitution, all three levels of government are jointly and severally liable (responsabilidade solidária) for health. [CF/88 Arts. 23(II), 196, 198]

Tema 793 (STF, 2019) refined this: while liability remains solidary, judges must direct compliance to the administratively responsible entity and order reimbursement between entities. Judges should include the responsible entity ex officio if not already named. [STF Tema 793]

Who actually pays

Municipalities bear the immediate fiscal burden in most cases [Fiocruz 2020, p. 463; CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 200]:

  • They are the easiest to sue (local court, local judges).
  • Smaller municipalities are frequently sued for treatments that are administratively the state's or federal government's responsibility. [Fiocruz 2020, p. 463]
  • Fund seizure (bloqueio) from municipal bank accounts is common. [Tjsp Caderno Judicializacao 2024, p. 1038; STJ Tema 84]

Reimbursement mechanisms (mostly post-2024):

  • Under Tema 1.234, for non-incorporated drugs costing 7–210 minimum salaries, the Union must reimburse 65% when a State or Municipality is condemned. [STF Tema 1.234 (2024)]
  • For oncology treatments filed before June 2024, the Union reimburses 80%. [STF Tema 1.234 (2024)]
  • Courts must cap reimbursement at the PMVG (maximum government price). [STF Tema 1.234 (2024)]
  • Pre-2024, no systematic reimbursement existed — municipalities bore the full cost. [Institutional fact; confirmed by absence of reimbursement mechanism prior to STF Temas 1.234/6 (2024)]

Cost premium

Judicial drug purchases are 30–44% more expensive than regular SUS procurement because they bypass competitive bidding processes. [Genicolo-Martins & Furquim de Azevedo 2024]

Fiscal mismatch

  • In the municipality of São Paulo, 55% of court-mandated pharmaceutical spending corresponded to medicines that were the responsibility of the state or federal government. [wang2014rap]

Budget impact

  • Total MoH judicial compliance costs: R$2.7 billion (2024), including pharmaceutical assistance (R$1.9B) and other compliance costs. Equivalent to the entire budget of Farmácia Popular. [folha2025elevidys]
  • Over 270 municipalities devoted between 30 and 100 percent of their health budgets to fulfilling judicial mandates in 2024. [theconversation2024]
  • Judicialized medications consume 8.4% of municipal pharmaceutical budgets on average (2023). [Ipea Gastos Judicializacao 2025, p. 545]
  • In 271 municipalities (2019–2023), total medication spending grew 30% while judicially mandated medication spending grew 22%. [Ipea Gastos Judicializacao 2025, p. 560]
  • In Campinas, judicial health spending reached 16% of the pharmaceutical budget in 2009; over half of the budget in some smaller municipalities. [diniz2014csc]
  • In a 2022 sample of 43 SP municipalities, 72% had tagged judicial health expenditures, with shares ranging from 0.0% to 6.8% of total health budgets. [Our TCE-SP data exploration, Feb 2025]
  • Formal budget reallocation evidence: TCE-SP Alterações Orçamentárias data (2015–2019) shows 34 of 644 municipalities creating explicit judicial budget lines (e.g., Santos: "MANDADOS JUDICIAIS / MED. E INSUMOS NAO PACTUADOS" under Suporte Profilático e Terapêutico, with zero initial dotação, receiving supplementary credits funded by anulação de dotação from other health lines). [Our TCE-SP Alterações Orçamentárias exploration, Feb 2026]
  • State-level judicialized medication spending reached 32.9% of total state pharmaceutical expenditures by 2023. [Ipea Gastos Judicializacao 2025, p. 545, 568]

6. Volume and Geographic Distribution

<!-- Updated: 2026-02-09 | Sources: TJSP data, CNJ/Insper 2019, Ipea 2025, tcu2017, vieira2020hhr, cambricoli2016estadao -->

Federal spending growth

  • Federal spending on judicial compliance for medicines rose thirteen-fold in seven years: R$70 million (2008) → over R$1 billion (2015). [tcu2017]
  • By 2024, reached R$1.9 billion (pharmaceutical assistance alone). [ipea2025]

Spending concentration

  • More than half of federal judicial health expenditures between 2010 and 2015 were concentrated in just three medicines not on the essential list. [vieira2020hhr]
  • Non-registered drugs (without ANVISA registration) accounted for R$545 million of MoH judicial compliance spending by 2015, up from R$2.4 million in 2010. [cambricoli2016estadao]

Caseload at TJSP

  • ~325,000 new health cases/year at TJSP: ~55% (~180,000) private health plans, ~37% (~120,000) SUS cases. [Sintese Tjsp Planos Vs Sus, p. 811]
  • Over 2005–2024, TJSP processed 1.29 million health sentenças across 3,006 judges and 322 comarcas. [TJSP full collapsed sentença data]
  • Growth: SUS cases grew from 2,317 (2008) to 23,465 (2017). [CNJ/Insper 2019, p. 87]
  • Post-2020, national 1st-instance public health case volume more than doubled: 76,836 (2020) → 162,046 (2024), a 110.9% increase. [Ipea Gastos Judicializacao 2025, p. 547]

Geographic distribution

  • São Paulo Capital: ~23.1% of health litigation. [TJSP sentença data]
  • Interior: ~76.9% across 321 comarcas (= 322 total minus capital). [TJSP sentença data] NOTE: Previous version said 313; corrected to 321 for arithmetic consistency with the 322 total. If 313 referred to a specific subset (e.g., comarcas with >N cases), this should be verified against the raw data.
  • Concentration in ABCD region (São Bernardo do Campo, Diadema, Santo André, Mauá), Campinas, Guarulhos, São José dos Campos. [Tjsp Sentencas Summary, p. 1363]
  • 100% of municipalities >500,000 inhabitants have active health litigation. [Ipea Gastos Judicializacao 2025, p. 556]
  • Among municipalities <5,000 inhabitants, 36.5% report providing medications due to court orders. [Ipea Gastos Judicializacao 2025, p. 555]

Relevance for our design

  • 77% interior matches TCE-SP coverage (644 municipalities, excludes SP capital under TCM-SP). [Our TCE-SP data exploration; TJSP sentença data]
  • Losing SP capital (~23% of caseload) is manageable for the municipal-level analysis.
  • Rapid growth provides time variation; jurisprudential shocks provide natural experiments.

7. Key Jurisprudential Milestones

<!-- Updated: 2026-02-09 | Sources: STF/STJ rulings, CNJ Res. 238, CNJ/Insper 2019, STF Pet 1246-SC, cnjpnud2025 -->

Origin of individual right to health

  • Pet 1246-SC (STF, 1997): Landmark decision ordering a state to fund treatment abroad for a child with a rare disease. Justice Celso de Mello held that "the individual right to life takes precedence over the financial and secondary interest of the State." This reasoning — treating the constitutional provision as an immediately enforceable individual entitlement to specific treatments — was rapidly adopted by lower courts across the country. [STF Pet 1246-SC; ferraz2009hhr; wang2015hepl]

Key rulings

These rulings changed the rules of health litigation and create natural variation in the data:

Year Ruling What it changed Source
1997 Pet 1246-SC (STF) Established individual right to health as immediately justiciable, not merely programmatic [STF Pet 1246-SC]
2016 CNJ Resolution 238 Created health-specialized varas and NAT-Jus system [CNJ Res. 238/2016]
2018 Tema 106 (STJ, REsp 1.657.156) Three cumulative requirements for non-RENAME drugs: (1) medical necessity + SUS ineffectiveness, (2) financial incapacity, (3) ANVISA registration. Applied from May 4, 2018. [STJ Tema 106]
2019 Tema 500 (STF) State generally cannot provide drugs without ANVISA registration [STF Tema 500]
2019 Tema 793 (STF) Solidary liability clarified: judges must direct compliance to administratively responsible entity [STF Tema 793]
2024 Tema 1.234 / Tema 6 (STF) Cost threshold: ≥210 min. salaries → Federal Court. Union reimburses 65%. Financial incapacity codified. [STF Temas 1.234, 6]
2024 SV 60 and 61 Binding: interfederative agreements, administrative exhaustion requirement before litigation [SV 60, 61]

Impact of Tema 106 on behavior: Minimal. Grant rates remained ~93%. Citations of technical standards in decisions: <6%. "Supply of medicines" filings: only 4% dip in 2017–2018. Volume continued to accelerate post-2020. [CNJ/Insper 2019]

Early evidence on 2024 reforms (SV 60/61, Temas 1.234/6): Modest effects so far. SUS-related lawsuits declined approximately 7% in the first half of 2025, but grant rates for preliminary injunctions remain above 73%. [cnjpnud2025]

Implication for our analysis: Pre-2024 data captures the period when (a) municipalities bore the full fiscal cost (no reimbursement), (b) there was no administrative exhaustion requirement, and (c) judges had maximum discretion.

8. Venue Choice and Forum Shopping

<!-- Updated: 2026-02-06 | Sources: CPC, TJSP Súmulas, STF Tema 793 | Fully sourced -->

Rules

  • No judge-shopping: automated random assignment within each comarca. [CPC Art. 284–285; NSCGJ]
  • Comarca = plaintiff's domicile (usually). [CPC Art. 46]
  • Entity-dimension forum shopping existed: plaintiffs could choose whether to sue the municipality, state, or Union, regardless of administrative responsibility. [CF/88 Art. 23(II); TJSP Súmula 37]
  • Tema 793 (2019) partially addressed this by requiring judges to redirect to the responsible entity. [STF Tema 793]
  • TJSP Súmula 37: Health action can be filed against any public entity. [TJSP Súm. 37]
  • TJSP Súmula 29: Prohibits denunciação da lide (third-party practice). [TJSP Súm. 29]

Implications for identification

  • Random assignment to judges is clean — no judge-shopping. [CPC Art. 284–285; NSCGJ/TJSP]
  • Forum shopping on the entity dimension does not threaten identification (it affects who pays, not which judge decides).
  • Comarca maps to municipality, supporting the case-to-municipality linkage. [CPC Art. 46; TJSP comarca organizational data]

Note on subfunção 122: Significant municipal health spending is reported under Administração Geral due to "matricialidade" rules, which capture administrative overhead of the health secretariat rather than direct care. [Portaria 42 Classification, p. 745] This requires attention in data cleaning.

Judicial spending is identifiable via free-text markers in historico_despesa: "conforme Determinação Judicial", "cumprimento de sentença", "tutela antecipada", "mandado de segurança", "ordem judicial", "decisão judicial", "liminar". [Our TCE-SP data exploration, Feb 2025]

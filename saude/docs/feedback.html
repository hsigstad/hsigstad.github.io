2026-02-08 — ChatGPT (o3) big-picture review

Context: Full draft (no results yet) reviewed for framing, general interest, contribution, and writing quality. Paper targets AER/QJE.

1. General interest and motivation

  • Opening hook (R$2.7b vs Farmácia Popular) is strong and legible to non-specialists.
  • Main critique: Paper still sells itself as "Brazil + health" rather than core political economy / public finance. Should frame as "courts as allocators under scarcity" — in the family of state capacity, earmarks/mandates, institutional design.
  • Tell broad audience what they learn even if they don't care about health: (i) reweighting toward high-cost idiosyncratic claims, (ii) rigidities that look like earmarks, (iii) vertical fiscal externalities, (iv) distributional incidence mediated by legal access.
  • "Why economists should care" paragraph reads like "this is a health policy debate." Recast as: the mode of allocation is an institution; institutions change marginal returns.

2. Contribution claims

Overselling:

  • "First causal estimates of judicial health spending on population health" invites literature-policing. Narrow to: "first causal estimates linking court-mandated health spending measured in budgets to population outcomes," or "first to quantify marginal health return of court-mandated vs administratively allocated spending."

Underselling:

  • Direct measurement of judicial spending in budget execution data is a real, distinctive contribution — push to front as headline.
  • Mechanism visibility (what gets crowded out: subfunções, procurement channel, primary care markers) turns "IV estimate" into "institutional welfare lesson." Emphasize more.
  • Judge-IV operating through municipal budgets + general-equilibrium spillovers is genuinely novel vs. individual-level leniency designs.

3. Framing and positioning

  • "Efficiency and equity of judicially mandated spending" is fine but not the most gripping top-5 frame.
  • Stronger frames: "Courts vs bureaucracies as allocation technologies" or "judicial mandates as earmarks under fiscal rigidity."
  • "Rights vs budgets" is rhetorically powerful but risks normative territory.
  • Lead with public finance / political economy (allocation, mandates, crowd-out, incidence), health econ and law & econ become inputs and mechanism interpretation.

4. Introduction

  • Hook works. Problem is pacing and redundancy after it.
  • Intro takes too long to get from "big facts" to "what we do that is new." Identification + measurement innovations should appear by paragraph 2.
  • Too many "this paper studies…" repetitions.
  • Suggested structure: (1) Hook + core tension. (2) One-sentence question + challenge + solution. Then three crisp contribution bullets. Move existing text forward and sharpen.

5. Related literature

  • Five-themes structure is defensible but reads like "we know the literature" rather than "here is the hole."
  • Reorder to match paper's identity:
    1. Courts and public resource allocation / mandates (bridge to general economics)
    2. Returns to spending and crowd-out / composition
    3. Health judicialization (institutional context)
    4. Distribution/incidence + litigation access
    5. Judge IV / leniency designs (short)
  • Judge-IV discussion is too long. AER/QJE referees don't need to be taught leniency designs; just say what's new about the application (budget aggregation + spillovers + mechanism measurement).
  • Connect more explicitly to mandates/earmarks/fiscal federalism as a mechanism literature.
  • Bring supply-side litigation organization earlier in the narrative.

6. Conceptual framework

  • Tripartite structure (efficiency, equity, spillovers) is right.
  • "Ex ante screening vs ex post adjudication" is the right backbone.
  • Risks "citation-driven theory" (law-review vibes). Cut number of canonical references in main text; move citation parade to footnotes.
  • Replace parts with one simple formal object: planner + courts + binding budget + heterogeneous litigation access → three predictions.
  • Build the whole framework around "court mandates as rigid, ex post earmarks" — maps cleanly to public finance and mechanism tests.

7. Institutional background

  • Overall calibration good. "Why budgets move" section is highly valuable.
  • Trim jurisprudence timeline: keep only what affects (i) discretionary variation exploited, (ii) who pays, (iii) whether sample is pre/post regime change. Rest to appendix.
  • Post-2024 reforms: one sentence ("our main period predates binding changes"), don't litigate legal details.

8. Writing quality

  • Above-average for early draft, not yet at "AER/QJE ruthless compression."
  • Redundancy: question and stakes restated too many times across intro and early sections.
  • Long multi-clause sentences: break them up.
  • Portuguese terms: decide convention — English in text, Portuguese in parentheses first time, then English.
  • Law-review tone risk: "maps onto competing predictions from economic theory" + many canonical references sounds like dignifying a policy debate rather than stating a crisp hypothesis.
  • Suggested: add a one-page roadmap figure (Courts → judicial spending → crowd-out/price premia → population outcomes + distribution).

9. Biggest risks for AER/QJE

  1. Perceived as Brazil/health-policy specialty paper. Fix: reframe first page around "allocation institutions under scarcity." If not, editor routes to health/development referees who treat it as field-journal material.
  2. Welfare/efficiency claim too ambitious for IV. Fix: be explicit — strongest claim is comparative marginal returns given observed crowd-out and procurement wedges, not "courts are bad/good."
  3. Equity narrative drowns out main contribution. Fix: treat litigant composition and supply-side as mechanisms that discipline interpretation, not as headline. Bring supply-side concentration evidence earlier.

Guiding principle: "Stop trying to win by breadth and win by a single sharp idea — allocation institutions change the marginal product and incidence of spending — and you have unusually direct measurement to show how."

2026-02-09 — ChatGPT (o3) individual-level data strategy

Context: Authors now have access to individual-level microdata on a secure server: SIH/SUS hospitalizations, SIM mortality (~2000–present), RAIS formal employment, and a complete Brazilian name registry (~50% unique nationally). Asked ChatGPT how this should reshape the empirical strategy. Paper PDF uploaded with prompt.

Core design recommendation: Two-tier design, municipality-level as spine

  • Tier 1 (population spillovers + fiscal mechanism): Keep the municipality-year (or municipality-month) 2SLS as the main estimand — effect of judge-induced judicial spending on population outcomes and budgets.
  • Tier 2 (direct effect on litigants): New section using case-level random judge assignment to estimate the effect of a grant on plaintiff health and labor-market trajectories.
  • Bridging piece: Use Tier 2 to interpret whether Tier 1 is negative/zero/positive because (i) direct gains are small, (ii) spillovers are negative (crowd-out), or (iii) both.

Key specification recommendations

  • Move from municipality-year to municipality-month/quarter where feasible (spending is at municipality-month)
  • For spillovers, construct non-litigant outcomes by excluding linked litigants from SIH/SIM
  • For litigant direct effects, use plaintiff-by-month panel around filing date with case-level judge IV
  • Aggregate for inference even when building from microdata — avoids Moulton problems
  • Cluster at court (vara) for population specs; at judge for litigant specs

Name-linkage selection

  • Selection affects external validity, not IV internal validity
  • Minimum required: selection table (linked vs unlinked on case/attorney/municipality characteristics)
  • IPW reweighting to full litigant composition (preferred over bounding)
  • Show results by strata of "name uniqueness propensity"
  • Name linkage only needed for litigant direct effects; population/spillover analyses don't need it

Highest-value analyses by data source

  • SIH: ICSAP by condition family, urgency split, age-gradient effects, municipality-month event studies
  • SIM: Amenable/avoidable mortality, cause-specific mortality, age-standardized rates
  • RAIS: Return-to-work/earnings for litigants; municipal employment as placebo/secondary
  • Name linkage: Pre-trends and trajectories, matched non-litigant comparisons, heterogeneity by case type

What stays at municipality level

  • Budget composition and subfunctions (Section 8.1)
  • ESF coverage, vaccination, prenatal (Section 8.2)
  • Fiscal capacity, revenue sources, budget rigidity

2026-02-09 — ChatGPT (o3) two-IV comparison strategy

Context: Follow-up to individual-data prompt. Asked whether the Section 6.4 comparison of returns to judicial vs administrative spending should be elevated to headline result, what instrument to use for administrative spending, and how individual data changes the comparison.

Headline vs benchmarking

  • Elevate to co-headline ("Courts vs bureaucracies as allocation technologies") only if the admin IV is credible and comparison directly addresses the two-LATE critique
  • Stays "benchmarking" if admin IV is messy or comparison is just "two LATEs, two point estimates"

Best instrument for administrative spending (ranked)

  1. Population-threshold RD/kink in transfer rules — cleanest if a sharp threshold exists
  2. Federal SUS transfer formula revisions (current plan) — can work if implemented with explicit policy timing, event studies, first-stage mapping to subfunctions, and falsification
  3. Revenue windfalls (oil royalties) — usually worse in SP setting (geographically concentrated, politically mediated)
  4. Constitutional floors (15% rule) — good as complement to turn a revenue shifter into health-specific first stage, not standalone
  5. Fundeb — education-finance architecture, hard to argue isolates health margin

The two-LATE problem: solution via two-endogenous-variable model

Instead of comparing two separate IV regressions, estimate:

$$Y_{mt} = \tau_J J_{mt} + \tau_A A_{mt} + \text{FE} + \varepsilon_{mt}$$

where J = judicial spending, A = administrative (non-judicial) spending, instrumented by judge leniency and transfer shock respectively. Reframes from "two LATEs" to "partial derivatives identified by two orthogonal shifters."

  • MTE framework (Mogstad-Torgovitsky) is overkill unless reviewers force it
  • Be transparent that policy margins differ, then show mechanism evidence pins down what each margin changes

Individual data improves the comparison by:

  • Making outcomes apples-to-apples (same cause-specific hospitalization/mortality definitions for both)
  • Enabling non-litigant-only judge-IV return — compare this to transfer-IV return for cleanest "courts vs bureaucracies" comparison
  • Litigant direct effects belong in decomposition/distributional sections, not in the allocation-technology comparison

Three numbers to report

  1. Marginal product of judicial spending (net population effect)
  2. Marginal product of administrative spending (transfer-IV)
  3. Non-litigant-only judge effect (spillover/crowd-out)

Framing recommendations

  • Frame as "allocation technologies under rigid budgets" — already in intro/conceptual framework
  • Translate to cost-effectiveness metrics: deaths averted per R$ million, ICSAP averted per R$ million
  • Tie comparison to mechanism evidence: different allocator → different spending composition → different marginal product
  • Do NOT frame as revealed-preference test or clean welfare ranking

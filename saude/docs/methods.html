Empirical Methods

Methods

Empirical Architecture: Two-Tier Design

Tier 1: Population-Level (Municipality-Period)

Unit: Municipality-period (month/quarter/year depending on outcome)

Endogenous variable: Per capita court-ordered health expenditure (J_mt), identified from TCE-SP text-tagged judicial transactions

Instrument: Aggregated leave-one-out judge leniency (Z̄_mt) — average of case-level instruments across all cases from municipality m in period t

Outcomes: Population health (SIH hospitalizations, SIM mortality, SINASC births), aggregated from individual-level microdata. Also: non-litigant outcomes (excluding linked litigants from SIH/SIM before aggregation)

Fixed effects: Court + period (baseline); court-by-period (robustness)

Clustering: Court (vara) level — the unit of random assignment

Extension — Two-endogenous-variable model:

  • Y_mt = τ_J J_mt + τ_A A_mt + FE + ε_mt
  • J instrumented by judge leniency; A (administrative spending) instrumented by federal SUS transfer formula variation
  • Reframes judicial vs administrative comparison from "two LATEs" to "partial derivatives from orthogonal shifters"

Tier 2: Litigant-Level (Plaintiff-by-Period Panel)

Unit: Plaintiff-by-period panel around filing date

Endogenous variable: Grant indicator (or granted cost) D_ijct

Instrument: Case-level leave-one-out judge leniency Z_ijct

Outcomes: Post-filing hospitalization (SIH), mortality (SIM), earnings and employment (RAIS)

Fixed effects: Court-by-filing-period

Clustering: Judge level

Name linkage: Deterministic unique-name matching via complete Brazilian name registry (~50% unique nationally). IPW reweighting for selection.

Decomposition

τ ≈ ω τ^L + (1-ω) τ^NL

  • τ^L: litigant effect (from Tier 2, scaled by litigant population share)
  • τ^NL: non-litigant effect (from non-litigant outcome specification)
  • ω: litigant share of population
  • Sign combinations discriminate corrective vs distortionary views

Individual-Level Data Pipeline

Microdata Sources

  • SIH/SUS: Universe of SUS hospitalizations — patient municipality, dates, ICD-10, age, sex, cost (~2000–present)
  • SIM: Universe of deaths — cause (ICD-10), municipality, date, demographics (~2000–present)
  • SINASC: All live births — birth weight, gestational age, prenatal visits, maternal characteristics
  • RAIS: Universe of formal employment — earnings, occupation, employer, duration (annual)
  • Name registry: Complete Brazilian name registry, ~50% nationally unique names

Name-Linkage Strategy

  • Baseline: Deterministic matching on exact unique full name within state-year
  • Sensitivity variants: (i) Jaro-Winkler fuzzy matching (>0.95), (ii) exact name + date of birth
  • Selection assessment: Linked vs unlinked comparison table; IPW reweighting; stratification by name-uniqueness propensity
  • Scope: Linkage required only for Tier 2 (litigant direct effects); Tier 1 does not require individual linkage

Outcome Construction

  • Municipality-period aggregation: From individual SIH/SIM records, aggregate to municipality-month or municipality-quarter
  • Cause-specific: ICD-10 enables ICSAP by condition family, amenable mortality, age-stratified rates
  • Non-litigant construction: Exclude linked litigant records from SIH/SIM before aggregation

Identification

Source of Variation

  • Random assignment of cases to judges within courts (varas da fazenda pública)
  • CNJ Resolution 238/2016: automated sequential distribution within designated health courts
  • Judges differ persistently in grant propensity (stringency)

Instrument Construction

  • Case-level: Z_ijct = leave-one-out average grant rate of judge j in court c at time t
  • Municipality-level: Z̄_mt = average of Z_ijct across cases from municipality m in period t

Key Threats

  1. Prevenção (related-case routing): Excluded from leave-one-out calculation
  2. Entity-dimension forum shopping: Does not affect within-court judge assignment
  3. Non-spending channels (timing, fines, bloqueio): Instrument captures stable tendency from other cases

Administrative Spending Instrument

Preferred candidate: FPM population-threshold fuzzy RD

  • FPM (Fundo de Participação dos Municípios): General federal transfer = 25.5% of IR + IPI, allocated to municipalities by population-bracket coefficients
  • Allocation rule: 18 population brackets (Decreto-Lei 1881/1981), coefficients from 0.6 to 4.0. Same bracket = same total FPM; crossing a threshold changes per capita transfers by ~20%
  • Thresholds (2023 values): 10,188; 13,584; 16,980; 23,772; 30,564; 37,356; 44,148; 50,940; 61,128; 71,316; 81,504; 91,692; 101,880; 115,464; 129,048; 142,632; 156,216
  • Identification: Fuzzy RD using IBGE population estimates as running variable (Corbi, Papaioannou & Surico 2019, REStud)
  • Channel: FPM → total municipal revenue → health spending (via 15% minimum floor + above-floor allocation choices) → administrative allocation across subfunctions
  • First stage needed: FPM coefficient jump → per capita health spending (not just total revenue)
  • Concern: FPM is general revenue, not health-specific. Exclusion restriction requires that FPM threshold effects on health outcomes operate only through health spending, not through education/infrastructure channels. Mitigation: control for non-health spending, or restrict to budget composition outcomes
  • Concern for SP: São Paulo state has highest fiscal autonomy in Brazil (76.6%). SP municipalities are less FPM-dependent than average — first stage may be weaker. Need to verify empirically

Alternative candidates

  • Previne Brasil 2019 reform: Changed primary care financing from PAB per capita (R$28/person) to capitation-weighted (R$50.50-131.30/person based on registered population and vulnerability). Sharp timing break, but only affects AB subfunção (~37% of spending), starts late in sample, and endogenous to ESF registration effort
  • SUS transfer formula portaria revisions: Portaria 204/2007 (financing blocks), Portaria 3.992/2017 (simplified to custeio + investimento). Create differential exposure across municipalities but harder to argue exogeneity
  • Parliamentary health amendments (EC 86/2015): 50% of individual congressional amendments must go to health. Political-cycle timing variation. Data availability uncertain

Key Variables

Budget Outcomes (TCE-SP)

  • Total judicial spending (text-tagged), by subfunction
  • Total health spending, by subfunction (301–306)
  • Administrative (non-judicial) spending = total minus judicial

Health Outcomes (from microdata)

  • ICSAP hospitalizations (total and by condition family)
  • Mortality (infant, under-5, amenable, cause-specific)
  • Birth outcomes (weight, prematurity, prenatal adequacy)
  • Age- and sex-standardized rates

Litigant Outcomes (linked microdata)

  • Post-filing hospitalization (SIH)
  • Post-filing mortality (SIM)
  • Post-filing earnings, employment indicator (RAIS)

Mechanism/Intermediate Outcomes

  • ESF coverage (teams per capita)
  • Vaccination coverage (PNI)
  • Prenatal care adequacy (SINASC)

Placebo Outcomes

  • Educational attainment (INEP School Census)
  • Formal employment at municipality level (RAIS)
  • Nighttime light intensity (VIIRS)

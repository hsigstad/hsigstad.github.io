Interfederative Responsibility

Interfederative Responsibility in Practice: Tema 793 and Downstream Behavior

Overview

This memo explains how the constitutional principle of federative solidarity in health (Art. 196, 198 CF/88) operates in practice, focusing on STF's Tema 793 and its implementation through administrative agreements and judicial behavior.

1. The Constitutional Framework

SUS Architecture

Three management levels:

  • Federal (União): National policy, high-cost drugs, strategic programs
  • State (Estados): Regional coordination, specialized care, most medications
  • Municipal (Municípios): Primary care, basic pharmacy, local services

Constitutional Principle (Art. 198)

"As ações e serviços públicos de saúde integram uma rede regionalizada e hierarquizada e constituem um sistema único."

Implications:

  • Unified system (sistema único)
  • But decentralized execution
  • Shared financing (federal, state, municipal)

2. Tema 793 (RE 855.178) - The Framework

The Problem Before Tema 793

Plaintiff's strategy:

Sue whoever is easiest: the Municipality (local court), the State (broader resources), or União (deepest pockets).

Result:

  • Forum shopping
  • Inconsistent decisions
  • Entities paying for obligations assigned to others
  • No reimbursement mechanism

Tema 793 Holdings

1. Solidarity affirmed:

"Os entes da Federação, em decorrência da competência comum, são solidariamente responsáveis nas demandas prestacionais na área da saúde."

2. BUT with internal allocation:

"A atribuição de competência não pode ser modificada por decisão judicial, devendo ser observada a pactuação realizada na Comissão Intergestores Tripartite (CIT)."

3. Reimbursement right:

"O ente condenado pode buscar ressarcimento junto ao ente efetivamente competente."

3. How Competence is Actually Allocated

The RENAME/PCDT System

Componente Básico da Assistência Farmacêutica (CBAF)

  • Responsibility: Primarily Municipal
  • Financing: Tripartite (União + Estado + Município)
  • Content: Basic essential drugs (antibiotics, antihypertensives, etc.)

Componente Estratégico da Assistência Farmacêutica (CESAF)

  • Responsibility: Federal → State distribution
  • Financing: Federal
  • Content: Endemic diseases, HIV, tuberculosis, vaccines

Componente Especializado da Assistência Farmacêutica (CEAF)

  • Responsibility: Shared (complex rules)
  • Financing: Tripartite with federal dominance
  • Content: High-cost drugs for chronic conditions

The Three Groups of CEAF

Group Financing Acquisition Distribution
1A Federal Federal State
1B Federal State State
2 State State State

Practical rule:

  • Groups 1A/1B → União ultimately responsible
  • Group 2 → State responsible
  • Basic → Municipality responsible

4. The CIT Resolutions

CIT (Comissão Intergestores Tripartite)

Composition: Ministry of Health + CONASS (states) + CONASEMS (municipalities)

Role: Agree on operational rules for SUS execution

Key Resolution: CIT nº 1/2021 (Consolidated)

Defines:

  • Which entity provides what
  • Financing flows
  • Reimbursement procedures
  • Dispute resolution

See: data/cit_resolucoes/resolucao-consolidacao-cit-01-2021.pdf

5. What Happens When Someone Sues the "Wrong" Entity

Scenario Analysis

Scenario 1: Patient sues Municipality for CEAF Group 1A drug

The drug is federal responsibility, but patient sued locally.

Old approach (pre-793):

Municipality condemned. No reimbursement. Municipality absorbs cost.

Post-793 approach:

Municipality condemned (solidarity), BUT:

  • Judge should identify União as ultimately responsible
  • Municipality has automatic right to seek reimbursement
  • Some courts now include União as litisconsorte

Scenario 2: Patient sues União for basic pharmacy drug

The drug is municipal responsibility.

Response:

União may be condemned (solidarity) but can:

  • Argue Municipality should be included
  • Seek reimbursement afterward

The Practical Problems

  1. Small municipalities can't pay:

    • Condemned for state/federal drugs
    • Can't afford to pay then seek reimbursement
    • Creates execution problems

  2. Reimbursement rarely works:

    • Bureaucratic process
    • Inter-entity disputes
    • Political reluctance

  3. Judges don't always apply correctly:

    • Some still condemn whoever is sued
    • Don't identify competent entity
    • Don't structure reimbursement

6. The 2024 Interfederative Agreements (Tema 1.234)

What Changed

Three agreements homologated by STF:

Agreement 1: Administrative Flow

  • Before suing, patient must request administratively
  • Standardized request forms
  • Response deadlines defined

Agreement 2: NAT-Jus Integration

  • Judges must consult NAT-Jus
  • Technical opinions guide decisions
  • Uniform criteria applied

Agreement 3: Reimbursement Mechanism

  • If State/Municipality condemned for federal drug → automatic reimbursement
  • Centralized tracking system
  • União responsible for CEAF 1A/1B drugs ordered judicially

Súmula Vinculante 60

"O pedido e a análise administrativos de fármacos na rede pública de saúde, a judicialização do caso, bem ainda seus desdobramentos (administrativos e jurisdicionais) devem observar os termos dos três acordos interfederativos..."

Effect: The agreements are now binding on everyone.

7. Downstream Behavior: How Entities Actually Respond

State Secretariats of Health

Common strategies:

  • Maintain "judicial pharmacy" units
  • Track litigation costs separately
  • Attempt administrative resolution before litigation
  • Seek União reimbursement for 1A/1B drugs

Challenges:

  • Staff overwhelmed by volume
  • Budget unpredictability
  • Difficulty proving "wrong entity" in court

Municipal Secretariats

Common strategies:

  • Refer complex cases to state
  • Argue lack of technical capacity
  • Emphasize budget constraints

Challenges:

  • Often can't afford lawyers
  • Smallest municipalities most vulnerable
  • Political pressure to resolve locally

União (Ministry of Health)

Common strategies:

  • Argue for strict competence rules
  • Emphasize CONITEC framework
  • Seek application of Tema 6 requirements

Challenges:

  • Largest target for high-cost drugs
  • Post-agreement reimbursement obligations
  • Managing national litigation volume

8. Judicial Behavior Patterns

Progressive Judges

Approach:

  • Grant medication immediately (solidarity)
  • Then determine competent entity
  • Order reimbursement if needed
  • Prioritize patient health

Reasoning:

"A solidariedade impõe o fornecimento imediato. A questão da competência será resolvida entre os entes."

Formalist Judges

Approach:

  • Verify competent entity first
  • May dismiss if wrong defendant
  • Strict Tema 793 application
  • Prioritize system organization

Reasoning:

"O autor deve demandar o ente competente conforme a pactuação federativa."

Post-2024 Trend

Increasing:

  • NAT-Jus consultation requirement
  • Administrative exhaustion verification
  • Correct entity identification
  • Tema 6 requirement scrutiny

Decreasing:

  • Automatic grants without analysis
  • Ignoring competence rules
  • Skipping technical consultation

9. Financing Flows and Budget Impact

How Judicial Orders Affect Budgets

Direct costs:

  • Drug acquisition
  • Storage and distribution
  • Staff time

Indirect costs:

  • Displacement of planned purchases
  • Emergency procurement (higher prices)
  • Administrative overhead

Budget Impact by Entity

União (2024 data):

  • R$ 2.7 billion in judicial medication orders
  • ~80% for non-incorporated drugs
  • Growing 15-20% annually

States (aggregate):

  • ~33% of pharmaceutical budgets from litigation
  • Varies widely by state (SP, RS highest)

Municipalities:

  • Harder to track
  • Estimated 5-15% of health budgets

10. Practical Guidance for Case Analysis

Determining the Competent Entity

Step 1: Is the drug incorporated?
├── Yes → Check RENAME/PCDT for which component
│   ├── Basic (CBAF) → Municipality
│   ├── Strategic (CESAF) → State (federal financing)
│   └── Specialized (CEAF)
│       ├── Group 1A → União (federal purchase)
│       ├── Group 1B → União (state purchase, federal $)
│       └── Group 2 → State
└── No → Apply Tema 6
    └── If granted → União (high-cost/non-incorporated)

Checklist for Interfederative Cases

  • Is drug incorporated in RENAME?
  • Which component (Basic/Strategic/Specialized)?
  • If CEAF, which group (1A/1B/2)?
  • Which entity was sued?
  • Is it the competent entity per CIT rules?
  • Should other entities be included (litisconsórcio)?
  • Is reimbursement mechanism applicable?
  • Was administrative request made (post-SV 60)?

11. Future Trends

Expected Developments

  1. Better tracking systems:

    • National database of judicial orders
    • Automated reimbursement processing
    • Real-time budget impact monitoring

  2. More structured litigation:

    • Standard administrative request forms
    • Mandatory NAT-Jus consultation
    • Clearer competence determination

  3. Pressure points:

    • Ultra-high-cost drugs (gene therapy)
    • Non-incorporated drugs with strong demand
    • Cross-border medication (importation)

Unresolved Issues

  1. What if CIT hasn't allocated a new drug yet?
  2. How to handle drugs that span components?
  3. Retroactive reimbursement for past orders?
  4. Municipal bankruptcy from health litigation?

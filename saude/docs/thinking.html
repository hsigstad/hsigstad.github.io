Open Questions & Ideas

Thinking and Open Ideas

Instrument for administrative spending

Core question

  • The two-endogenous-variable model needs an instrument for administrative (non-judicial) health spending A_mt
  • The instrument must be: (a) exogenous to municipal health demand, (b) correlated with health spending, (c) excludable from health outcome equation conditional on J_mt
  • Three candidate families identified (see below)
  • Key open question: does the 15% minimum floor bind? If municipalities are well above it, FPM shocks to total revenue may not translate to health spending changes. CONASEMS data (Table 2.2) shows municipalities spend 20-25% of own revenue on health (well above 15%), suggesting the floor rarely binds — but the marginal dollar from FPM could still affect health spending through the composition channel (which subfunctions get the marginal revenue?)

FPM vs SUS-specific transfers

  • FPM is a general fiscal transfer — instrument for total municipal revenue, not health-specific
  • SUS fundo-a-fundo transfers (PAB, MAC, etc.) are health-specific but allocation formulas are complex and have changed over time
  • Which is better for our design?
    • FPM: cleaner identification (population thresholds → fuzzy RD), well-established in literature (Corbi et al. 2019 REStud), but indirect channel to health spending
    • SUS transfers: more direct health spending channel, but allocation is discretionary/formula-based without clean thresholds
    • The Previne Brasil 2019 reform is a potential instrument (sharp timing, capitation-based), but only affects primary care and starts late in our sample

Interaction with 15% minimum floor

  • Court-ordered spending counts toward the 15% minimum (it's health expenditure)
  • So judicial mandates may crowd out administrative health spending within the 15% bucket rather than forcing municipalities above the floor
  • This is testable: do municipalities near the floor have larger crowd-out effects?

Candidate instruments

Candidate A: FPM population-threshold RD (preferred)

  • Strengths: clean identification, established literature, strong first stage
  • Use as instrument for total administrative health spending (health spending net of judicial mandates)
  • Logic: FPM → total municipal revenue → health spending (via 15% floor and above-floor choices) → administrative allocation across subfunctions
  • Weakness: indirect channel; need to demonstrate first stage specifically for health spending, not just total revenue

Candidate B: Previne Brasil 2019 reform

  • Sharp timing change in primary care financing formula (from PAB per capita to capitation-weighted)
  • Affects only AB (Atenção Básica) subfunção — ~37% of municipal health spending
  • Could instrument specifically for primary care administrative spending
  • Weakness: late in sample (2019+), concurrent with COVID (2020+), endogenous to ESF team registration

Candidate C: PAB-fixo population estimate revisions

  • Before Previne Brasil (2019), PAB-fixo was a flat ~R$28/person/year transfer to all municipalities
  • Allocated using IBGE population estimates, recalibrated sharply after each Census
  • Census population revisions create exogenous shocks to primary care transfers specifically
  • Same identification logic as FPM (population-based) but health-earmarked
  • Weakness: uses essentially the same variation as FPM (IBGE population estimates); small magnitude compared to total health spending

Candidate D: SUS transfer formula revisions

  • Multiple portarias changed federal health transfer rules over our sample period
  • Portaria GM/MS 204/2007 (financing blocks), Portaria 3.992/2017 (simplified to 2 blocks: custeio + investimento)
  • These create differential exposure across municipalities depending on pre-reform spending composition
  • Weakness: harder to argue exogeneity; municipalities may have anticipated/lobbied for changes

Candidate E: MAC ceilings (Teto MAC)

  • Federal reimbursement ceilings for medium/high complexity care set by PPI/CIR negotiations
  • If there are formulaic or administrative revision events that shift ceilings discontinuously, could instrument hospital-spending variation
  • Weakness: likely endogenous to installed hospital capacity and political negotiation

Candidate F: Oil/mining royalties (Compensações Financeiras)

  • Windfall municipal revenue from natural resource extraction
  • Exogenous to health demand, affects health spending via 15% floor
  • Weakness: likely irrelevant for most SP municipalities (no significant oil/mining)

Candidate G: 2022 Census population revision shock

  • IBGE Census published 2023; 863 municipalities nationally had FPM coefficients reduced
  • Sharp, one-time exogenous shock to transfers
  • Weakness: very late in our sample period, one-time cross-sectional event

Assessment

  • FPM-RD is the strongest in terms of identification clarity and precedent in the literature
  • Health-specific alternatives (PAB, MAC) are either endogenous or use the same variation as FPM
  • The main open question is empirical: does FPM have a strong enough first stage for health spending in SP specifically?
  • A complementary approach: use heterogeneity in distance from the 15% floor — not as a second instrument, but to sharpen interpretation. Municipalities barely above 15% should show larger crowd-out from judicial mandates than those at 25%. This tests the budget-constraint mechanism without needing a second instrument

Related literature

Corbi, Papaioannou & Surico (2019, REStud) — "Regional Transfer Multipliers"

  • Uses FPM population thresholds for fuzzy RD in Brazil
  • FPM accounts for ~80% of federal transfers and ~31% of total municipal revenues
  • 18 population brackets with coefficients from 0.6 to 4.0
  • At each threshold, per capita transfers jump ~20% (municipalities in adjacent brackets receive ~20% more/less per capita)
  • Key results: local income multiplier ~2, cost per job ~$8,000/year, effects mainly through services employment, larger effects in less financially developed municipalities
  • DOES NOT examine health spending specifically — focuses on employment and income
  • Our contribution: apply same identification to health spending composition and health outcomes

Previne Brasil (Portaria 2.979/2019)

  • Replaced PAB (Piso de Atenção Básica) with capitation-weighted model
  • Four components: (i) weighted capitation, (ii) payment for performance, (iii) strategic action incentives, (iv) population-based financial incentives
  • Per capita: R$50.50/year base, up to R$131.30/year with vulnerability weighting
  • Weights: socioeconomic vulnerability, demographic profile, geographic location
  • Key shift: from automatic per capita (everyone gets same) to registered population (must register patients in ESF teams)
  • Potential sharp break in primary care financing — but endogenous to ESF registration effort

SUS financing constitutional framework

  • EC 29/2000: established minimum spending floors (12% states, 15% municipalities)
  • LC 141/2012: regulated the floors, defined ASPS (health expenditure categories), established rateio (allocation) criteria for federal transfers
  • EC 86/2015: changed federal minimum from "prior year + GDP growth" to "15% of RCL" (phased from 13.2% in 2016)
  • EC 95/2016 (Teto de Gastos): froze federal spending at 2016 levels + IPCA, estimated R$654bn loss to SUS over 20 years
  • EC 113/2021: modified spending ceiling
  • EC 126/2022 (PEC da Transição): ended spending ceiling
  • LC 200/2023: new fiscal framework, restored 15% of RCL for health
  • These changes create macro-level federal spending shocks but affect all municipalities equally — not useful as cross-sectional instruments

Municipal health spending composition (CONASEMS 2025)

  • Two dominant subfunctions: AB (Atenção Básica) 37% and AHA (Assistência Hospitalar Ambulatorial) 43%
  • Administrative costs: 13%
  • Surveillance/other: ~7%
  • Municipal fiscal autonomy: only ~9% of revenue from own taxes; ~84-87% from transfers
  • FPM is the dominant transfer for non-capital, non-metropolitan municipalities
  • Municipalities spend well above 15% floor (average 20-25%), suggesting floor rarely binds
  • São Paulo state: 76.6% fiscal autonomy (highest in Brazil) — so SP municipalities are less transfer-dependent than average

Methodological sketches

FPM fuzzy RD design for administrative health spending

  • Running variable: municipal population (IBGE estimates, used by TCU for coefficient assignment)
  • Treatment: FPM coefficient (step function with 18 thresholds)
  • First stage: FPM coefficient → per capita FPM transfer → per capita total revenue → per capita health spending (or specific subfunctions)
  • Fuzzy RD because: (a) coefficient assignment is based on IBGE population estimates (not exact census counts), (b) multiple thresholds can be pooled (Corbi et al. pool 17 thresholds using normalized running variable)
  • Key question for our sample: do SP municipalities cluster around thresholds? (Most SP municipalities are in the 10k-150k range, which spans most thresholds)
  • Need to check: do judicial spending shocks affect population reporting? (Likely not — population estimates are from IBGE, unrelated to health litigation)

Combining FPM-RD with judge-IV in a single framework

  • Y_mt = τ_J * J_mt + τ_A * A_mt + X_mt'β + α_m + γ_t + ε_mt
  • Instrument J_mt with leave-one-out judge leniency Z_mt
  • Instrument A_mt with FPM transfer shock (distance from threshold × coefficient jump)
  • Requires: (a) both instruments have strong first stages, (b) exclusion restrictions hold for each instrument conditional on the other endogenous variable
  • The exclusion restriction for the FPM instrument is: FPM transfers affect health outcomes only through administrative health spending, not through other channels
    • Concern: FPM also funds education, infrastructure, etc. — could affect health through non-health channels (e.g., sanitation, education)
    • Mitigation: control for non-health spending, or argue that within-municipality variation in FPM (from threshold jumps) is small relative to total budget, so cross-spending contamination is second-order
    • Or: restrict to health-specific subfunctions as dependent variable (budget composition rather than health outcomes)

Alternative: stacked threshold-specific RD

  • Instead of pooling, estimate separate RD at each FPM threshold relevant for SP municipalities
  • Advantage: transparent, allows heterogeneity by municipality size
  • Disadvantage: small samples at each threshold

Ideas to explore

  • Can we use SIOPS (Sistema de Informações sobre Orçamentos Públicos em Saúde) data to separate federal transfers to municipal health funds from own-source health spending? This decomposition would let us instrument federal health transfers separately from own-source spending
  • The Portaria 3.992/2017 reform (consolidating financing blocks into custeio + investimento) could create a natural experiment in municipal spending flexibility — municipalities that were previously constrained by block-specific rules got more discretion. Potential diff-in-diff based on pre-reform binding constraints
  • Parliamentary amendments (emendas parlamentares) earmarked for health (50% of individual amendments must go to health since EC 86/2015) — could be another source of exogenous health spending variation, potentially with political-cycle timing
  • The IPEA working paper (1818) on "FPM e a Estranha Distribuição da População dos Pequenos" examines population bunching at FPM thresholds — important for validity of the RD design
  • FUNDEB education transfers could free up own-source revenue for health by crowding in education spending — indirect instrument, but FUNDEB has its own formulaic allocation rules
  • Shift-share design using Portaria 3.992/2017 reform: municipalities with different pre-reform financing-block compositions got differential flexibility shocks when blocks were consolidated into custeio + investimento
  • Could the two-endogenous-variable model work without a second instrument? If we're willing to assume administrative spending is exogenous conditional on FE (weaker but possibly defensible for some subfunctions like AB that are formula-driven), we could estimate the model with one instrument and one "control variable" rather than two instruments

Notes

  • São Paulo state has the highest fiscal autonomy in Brazil (76.6% of receipts from own taxes) — this means SP municipalities are LESS dependent on FPM than average Brazilian municipality. FPM instrument may be weaker in SP. Need to check first-stage strength
  • All municipalities in our sample (321 comarcas in SP excluding capital) are likely in the 10k-500k population range, covering most FPM thresholds
  • The CONASEMS data shows municipalities averaged R$42.2 million in total health spending in 2022 — the average AB spending per municipality was R$16 million (37% of total). This gives a sense of the scale: judicial spending (our J_mt) would need to be a meaningful fraction of this for the two-endogenous-variable model to work

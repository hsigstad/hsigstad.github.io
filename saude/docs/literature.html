STRAND 1: Health Judicialization in Brazil (Empirical)

The empirical Brazil literature documents who litigates and what is demanded (often medicines/ICU beds), with substantial heterogeneity across places and institutions. A newer empirical strand studies institutional responses (e.g., NATJUS technical notes) and their impacts.

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Finding Relevance
Diniz, Machado & Penalva (2014) The judicialization of health in the Federal District of Brazil Ciência & Saúde Coletiva Case-level analysis of 385 lawsuits finds ICU access and drugs prominent, with most cases filed by public defenders. Baseline facts on case mix and Defensoria role.
Biehl, Petryna, Amon & Socal (2016) The Judicialization of Health and the Quest for State Accountability Health and Human Rights, 18(2) Systematic lawsuit sample shows heterogeneous claims and challenges "elite capture" narratives. Closest Brazil micro-empirics on medicines litigation.
Pepe et al. (2010) Characterization of lawsuits for the supply of "essential" medicines… Rio de Janeiro Cadernos de Saúde Pública, 26(3) 185 suits for "essential" medicines in RJ; claims granted in nearly all cases. Direct evidence on grant rates and on-list medicine litigation.
Figueiredo et al. (2013) Evidence-based process for decision-making in the analysis of drug requests Cadernos de Saúde Pública Analyzes medicines demanded through lawsuits in Rio (2007-2008). Benchmarks what is litigated (drug types).
Wang & Ferraz (2013) Reaching out to the needy? Access to justice and public attorneys' role SUR – International Journal on Human Rights Access-to-justice institutions shape the socioeconomic profile of plaintiffs. Directly relevant for "who litigates?" in São Paulo.
Peçanha et al. (2020) Judicialization of medicines in the State of Rio de Janeiro Saúde em Debate Court-database evidence (2010-2017) shows broad geographic growth in medicine lawsuits. Trend/scale and municipal heterogeneity in volumes.
Machado et al. (2011) Judicialization of access to medicines in Minas Gerais Revista de Saúde Pública Systematic characterization of drug lawsuits in MG state. State-level case mix evidence.
Oliveira et al. (2020) Judicialização de medicamentos: efetividade de direitos ou ruptura na equidade? Revista de Saúde Pública Questions whether litigation promotes or undermines equity. Distributional concern.
Oliveira et al. (2025) The High Cost of the Legal Route in Public Health Lancet Regional Health – Americas Medication litigation imposes large fiscal burdens concentrated in few beneficiaries. Closest competitor on magnitude/incidence of costs.
Correia et al. (2025) Impact evaluation of technical notes issued by NATJUS PubMed Central Evaluates economic impact of NATJUS technical notes on judicial decisions. Court-side "evidence tech" potentially shifting spending.
Genicolo-Martins & Furquim de Azevedo (2024) Bitter Pills to Swallow: The Enforcement Costs of Health Litigation Working Paper (Insper) Judicial enforcement leads to 30-44% higher pharmaceutical procurement prices and fewer bidding firms in urgent tenders vs. ordinary ones. Causal evidence on procurement cost channel; complements our health outcomes focus.
CNJ/INSPER (2019) Judicialização da Saúde no Brasil: Perfil das Demandas, Causas e Propostas de Solução CNJ Report Health litigation grew 130% (2008-2017); 498,715 first-instance cases; federal spending reached R$1.6 billion by 2016. Official diagnostic with national scale/trend data.

Gap: Rich micro-descriptives, but very little causal evidence linking judicially induced spending to population health outcomes at scale.

STRAND 2: Health Judicialization Outside Brazil

Cross-country work emphasizes that rights litigation can expand access but may create budgetary and priority-setting tensions. Quantitative causal evidence on health outcomes remains scarce.

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Finding Relevance
Mæstad, Rakner & Ferraz (2011) Assessing the impact of health rights litigation (comparative) Chapter in Yamin & Gloppen Comparative assessment of litigation's effect on priority-setting. Structured comparison for positioning Brazil vs. other regimes.
Yamin & Gloppen (eds., 2011) Litigating health rights Harvard University Press Collects country studies including Colombia's tutela system. External-validity discussion anchor.
Arrieta-Gómez (2018) Realizing the Fundamental Right to Health through Litigation Health and Human Rights Colombia's judicial intervention shaping health system compliance. Mechanisms/constraints in Latin American rights enforcement.
Yamin (2010) Judicial protection of the right to health in Colombia Hastings Int'l & Comparative Law Review Colombia's tutela system and health rights. Detailed Colombia case study.
Andia & Lamprea (2019) Is the judicialization of health care bad for equity? A scoping review BMJ Global Health Reviews cross-country evidence on equity effects of health litigation. Directly relevant equity question.
Loaiza et al. (2018) Revisiting Health Rights Litigation in Costa Rica Health and Human Rights Costa Rica's Sala IV and medication access. Latin American comparator.
Norheim & Wilson (2014) Health Rights Litigation and Access to Medicines: Priority Setting in Costa Rica Working paper Priority-setting tensions from rights litigation. Institutional design comparison.

Gap: Outside-Brazil literature is often comparative/institutional; our contribution is a causal municipal panel estimate.

STRAND 3: Courts, Rights, and Resource Allocation — US and Comparative

US-centered canon: courts shift public spending via mandates (school finance reforms are cleanest example), with measurable long-run outcomes.

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Finding Relevance
Jackson, Johnson & Persico (2016) The Effects of School Spending on Educational and Economic Outcomes QJE, 131(1), 157-218 Court-mandated reforms increase K-12 spending and improve education/earnings. Closest US analog: court-induced spending → long-run outcomes.
Lafortune, Rothstein & Schanzenbach (2018) School Finance Reform and the Distribution of Student Achievement NBER WP 22011 Court-mandated formula changes raise achievement in low-income districts. Why where the money goes matters.
Hanushek, Joyce & Wirtz (2023) Incidence and Outcomes of School Finance Litigation Public Finance Review Comprehensive analysis of school finance litigation outcomes. US court mandate literature.
Anstreicher, Fletcher & Thompson (2022) The Long Run Impacts of Court-Ordered Desegregation NBER WP 29926 Desegregation orders had lasting effects on outcomes. Court mandates with long-run consequences.
Borgschulte & Vogler (2020) Did the ACA Medicaid Expansion Save Lives? Journal of Health Economics Medicaid expansion (partly litigated) reduced mortality. Health spending expansion via legal/policy channel.

Gap: US court-mandate work largely studies education; we provide analogous design for health spending mandates.

STRAND 4: Judge IV / Examiner Design — Methodology and Applications

Judge and examiner IV designs are now a mature empirical strategy, with recent work formalizing identification assumptions, many-instrument issues, and interpretation of LATEs. Applications have expanded far beyond criminal justice into patents, immigration, disability insurance, and child welfare.

Core methodology and diagnostics

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Finding Relevance
Chyn, Frandsen & Leslie (2025) Examiner and Judge Designs in Economics: A Practitioner's Guide Journal of Economic Literature, 63(2) Synthesizes identification, implementation, and diagnostics. Must-cite blueprint for presenting judge-IV design.
Frandsen, Lefgren & Leslie (2023) Judging Judge Fixed Effects American Economic Review, 113(1) Derives testable implications for exclusion/monotonicity. Canonical reference for defending judge-IV assumptions.
Coulibaly, Hsu, Mourifié & Wan (2024) A Sharp Test for the Judge Leniency Design NBER WP 32456 Sharp specification test for random assignment, exclusion, and monotonicity. Modern validity test for empirical appendix.
Goldsmith-Pinkham, Hull & Kolesár (2025) Leniency Designs: An Operator's Manual NBER WP 34473 Systematizes leniency IV designs and implementation. Aligned with LOO judge stringency construction.
Goldsmith-Pinkham, Hull & Kolesár (2022) Contamination Bias in Linear Regressions Review of Economic Studies Shows bias from correlated instruments across units. Relevant for clustered assignment environments.
Hull (2018) Instrumental Variables with Heterogeneous Treatment Effects Quantitative Economics Clarifies IV estimands under heterogeneity. LATE interpretation of judicial spending.
Kolesár et al. (2015) Identification and Inference with Many Invalid Instruments JBES Inference under many instruments and weak identification. Relevant given many-judge environments.
Jochmans (2023) Many (Weak) Judges in Judge-Leniency Designs TSE Working Paper Studies asymptotics when many judges have small caseloads. Justifies inference/estimator choices.

Applications outside criminal justice

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Finding Relevance
Sampat & Williams (2019) How Do Patents Affect Follow-On Innovation? American Economic Review Patent examiner leniency as instrument for patent grants. Clean non-criminal examiner-IV benchmark.
French & Song (2014) The Effect of Disability Insurance Receipt on Labor Supply AEJ: Economic Policy Examiner assignment in SSDI identifies causal effects. Canonical examiner-IV outside courts.
Dahl, Kostøl & Mogstad (2014) Family Welfare Cultures Quarterly Journal of Economics Quasi-random caseworker assignment in welfare. Related design logic for bureaucratic discretion.
Arnold, Dobbie & Yang (2018) Racial Bias in Bail Decisions Quarterly Journal of Economics Judge IV studying pretrial detention effects. Shows judge IV credibility at top journals.
Mueller-Smith (2015) The Criminal and Labor Market Impacts of Incarceration Working paper Judge IV estimating incarceration impacts. Benchmark for scale + long-run outcomes.
Norris (2023) Examiner Inconsistency: Evidence from Refugee Appeals Working paper Identification and inference with many examiners. Directly relevant for many-judge environment.

Gap: Judge/examiner IV has not yet been used to estimate health-system-level spending shocks and population health outcomes, especially in a middle-income country with constitutional health rights.

STRAND 5: Returns to Public Health Spending

Causal evidence overwhelmingly shows that primary care and preventive spending yield large mortality and morbidity gains, particularly in low- and middle-income settings. By contrast, marginal returns to hospital- or pharmaceutical-heavy spending are less well established.

Brazil-focused causal evidence

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Finding Relevance
Rocha & Soares (2010) Evaluating the impact of community-based health interventions Health Economics, 19(S1) FHP rollout reduces mortality across ages. Core benchmark for public health resources → outcomes.
Aquino et al. (2009) Impact of the Family Health Program on Infant Mortality American Journal of Public Health FHP expansion reduces infant mortality. Baseline benchmark for Brazilian PHC returns.
Rasella et al. (2014) Impact of primary health care on mortality from CVD in Brazil BMJ, 349 Greater FHP coverage reduces CVD-related mortality. Connects primary care to ACSC-like outcomes (ICSAP).
Rasella et al. (2013) Effect of a Conditional Cash Transfer Programme on Childhood Mortality Lancet Bolsa Família + PHC interactions reduce mortality. Shows complementarities with PHC spending.
Bhalotra, Rocha & Soares (2019) Does Universalization of Health Work? IZA DP 12111 Health systems restructuring and expansion reduce mortality. Causal evidence on SUS expansion.
Bastos et al. (2017) Effect of Primary Care on Hospitalizations for ACSC PLOS ONE PHC reduces avoidable hospitalizations. Directly tied to ICSAP outcome.
Hone et al. (2017) PHC expansion and racial inequalities in mortality PLOS Medicine PHC expansion reduces mortality and narrows racial inequalities. Relevant for distributional angle.
Hone et al. (2023) Primary healthcare and infant health outcomes Lancet Regional Health – Americas PHC coverage associated with improved infant outcomes. Justifies birth outcomes as key endpoint.
Pinto et al. (2024) The impact of primary health care on AIDS incidence and mortality PLOS Medicine Higher FHS coverage reduces AIDS incidence/mortality. PHC generates large health returns — supports crowd-out narrative.

Cross-country and budget-shock evidence

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Finding Relevance
Filmer & Pritchett (1999) The Impact of Public Spending on Health Social Science & Medicine Weak average correlation between spending and outcomes. Motivation for composition-focused analysis.
Moreno-Serra & Smith (2015) Broader Health Coverage Is Good for the Nation's Health JRSS: Series A Universal coverage expansions improve outcomes. Spending vs coverage distinction.
Bokhari, Gai & Gottret (2007) Government Health Expenditures and Health Outcomes Health Economics Government health spending reduces mortality. Cross-country benchmark.
Clemens & Miran (2022) Fiscal Policy, Sovereign Debt, and Austerity QJE Government spending effects depend on composition. High-level fiscal framing.
Finkelstein, Hendren & Luttmer (2019) The Value of Medicaid JPE Insurance expansions reduce mortality and improve welfare. Mechanism comparator.

Gap: Existing work estimates returns to programmatic expansions; we estimate returns to judicially induced, non-programmatic spending, which may differ sharply in effectiveness.

STRAND 6: Distributional Effects of Public Spending and Access to Justice

Benefit-incidence studies show that public health spending can be progressive on average, but access to justice may skew who captures marginal benefits. Legal institutions therefore matter for the distributional incidence of social rights.

Distributional incidence

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Finding Relevance
Higgins, Lustig, Ruble & Smeeding (2016) Comparing the Incidence of Taxes and Social Spending in Brazil and the US Review of Income and Wealth Comprehensive fiscal incidence comparing Brazil/US including health. Framework for whether judicialization is regressive/progressive.
Lustig, Lopez-Calva & Ortiz-Juarez (2013) Declining Inequality in Latin America World Development Social spending substantially reduces inequality in LAC. Baseline incidence framework.
Higgins & Lustig (2016) Can a Poverty-Reducing Tax and Transfer System Hurt the Poor? Journal of Development Economics Some transfers can hurt subsets of the poor. Cautionary distributional lens.
Paes de Barros et al. (2009) Measuring Inequality of Opportunities in LAC World Bank Documents inequality dynamics in Brazil. Background for equity discussion.
O'Donnell et al. (2008) Analyzing Health Equity Using Household Survey Data World Bank Methods for benefit incidence in health. Methodological reference.

Access to justice and legal aid

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Finding Relevance
Gauri & Brinks (2008) Courting Social Justice Cambridge University Press Courts can expand rights but risk elite capture. Canonical framing.
Wang (2015) Courts as Healthcare Policymakers Health Economics, Policy and Law Courts shape allocation through individual cases. Conceptual bridge to our design.
Gloppen (2006) Courts and Social Transformation Journal of Human Rights Courts' distributive role depends on institutional design. Helps interpret heterogeneity in effects.

Gap: We provide causal evidence on whether judicial access reallocates health resources toward or away from poorer populations.

STRAND 7: Crowd-Out and Resource Allocation in Health

Health budgets are often rigid, so mandated increases in one category can crowd out others. Evidence from austerity, earmarks, and intergovernmental transfers shows substantial reallocation effects.

Health-system crowd-out

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Finding Relevance
Cutler & Gruber (1996) Does Public Insurance Crowd Out Private Insurance? QJE Public insurance crowds out private coverage. Classic crowd-out mechanism.
Currie & Gruber (1996) Health Insurance Eligibility and Child Health QJE Public insurance eligibility improves child health but crowds out private. Mechanism analog.
McKnight (2006) Home Care Reimbursement, Long-Term Care Utilization, and Health Outcomes Journal of Health Economics Reimbursement changes affect care utilization. Demand-side spillovers.
Hines & Thaler (1995) The Flypaper Effect Journal of Economic Perspectives Grants stick where they hit. Fiscal allocation logic.
Litschig & Morrison (2012) Intergovernmental Transfers and Education Outcomes AEJ: Applied Economics Transfers change spending composition. Analogous budget mechanism.
Marinho (2024) Crowding-Out Effects of a Universal Primary Care Policy SSRN FHP expansion reduces private plan coverage. Public provision shocks reallocate coverage.

Austerity and fiscal stress

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Finding Relevance
Maia et al. (2024) Fiscal austerity and municipal health spending Revista de Saúde Pública Effects of austerity on municipal health spending patterns. Budget constraint environment where court mandates can crowd out.
O'Dwyer et al. (2019) Rio de Janeiro fiscal crisis Ciência & Saúde Coletiva Fiscal stress reduces PHC and hospital provision. How fiscal stress transmits to service mix.
Rasella et al. (2018) Child morbidity and mortality from austerity PLOS Medicine Austerity increases child morbidity/mortality. Reallocations away from protective spending worsen outcomes.

Brazil-specific fiscal rigidity

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Finding Relevance
Vieira & Zucchi (2016) Pharmaceutical Assistance in SUS Ciência & Saúde Coletiva Pharma spending increasingly rigid. Explains why court mandates crowd out PHC.
Funcia & Ocke-Reis (2018) Underfunding of SUS Saúde em Debate Chronic budget constraints. Institutional constraint.
Piola et al. (2013) Health Financing in Brazil IPEA Documents earmarks and rigidity. Fiscal context.

Gap: No existing paper causally links judicial mandates to crowd-out and downstream health outcomes.

STRAND 9: Economic Theory Frameworks

Formal economic theory generating testable predictions for judicial mandates and health spending. See docs/theory.md for full mapping of theories to predictions and empirical design.

Welfare economics and rights

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Result Relevance
Kaplow & Shavell (2001) Fairness versus Welfare Harvard Law Review, 114(4) Any non-welfarist rule giving independent weight to fairness/rights can be Pareto-dominated by a welfarist rule. Rights-based health mandates that ignore opportunity costs are welfare-dominated unless instrumentally justified.
Kaplow & Shavell (2002) Fairness versus Welfare Harvard University Press Book-length treatment of the above. Extended argument and responses to critics.

Information, allocation, and mechanism design

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Result Relevance
Hayek (1945) The Use of Knowledge in Society AER, 35(4) Dispersed local knowledge favors decentralized allocation; centralization fails when information is tacit and fast-changing. Courts may elicit local information (severity, urgency) agencies miss; but courts miss systemic trade-offs.
Myerson (1979) Incentive Compatibility and the Bargaining Problem Econometrica, 47(1) Efficient allocation requires incentive-compatible mechanisms eliciting private information truthfully. Courts as ex post mechanisms with weak IC (agents exaggerate need) vs. agencies designing ex ante screening.
Maskin (1999) Nash Equilibrium and Welfare Optimality Review of Economic Studies, 66(1) Characterizes when social choice rules can be implemented in Nash equilibrium. Formal conditions for when centralized vs. decentralized mechanisms achieve welfare optima.
Weitzman (1974) Prices vs. Quantities Review of Economic Studies, 41(4) Quantities preferred when marginal benefit uncertainty dominates; prices preferred when marginal cost uncertainty dominates. Court orders (price-like: must fund regardless) vs. health budgets (quantity instruments). Mandates efficient for rare/urgent claims, inefficient when costs explode.

Health capital

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Result Relevance
Grossman (1972) On the Concept of Health Capital and the Demand for Health JPE, 80(2) Health as depreciating capital stock; individuals invest until MC = MB; diminishing returns fundamental. Court mandates at high-cost margins (flat of production function) yield lower returns than inframarginal PHC investments.

Optimal earmarking and public finance

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Result Relevance
Buchanan (1963) The Economics of Earmarked Taxes JPE, 71(5) Earmarking restricts budget flexibility; equivalent to lump-sum transfers under full fungibility. Judicial mandates as rigid ex post earmarks — inefficient if budgets are fungible.
Brett & Keen (2000) Political Uncertainty and the Earmarking of Environmental Taxes Journal of Public Economics, 75(3) Earmarking can be optimal when voters face fiscal illusion. Conditions for when judicial earmarks improve allocation.
Dhillon & Perroni (2001) Tax Earmarking and Grass-Roots Accountability Economics Letters, 72(1) Earmarking as commitment device under political commitment problems. Conditions for when judicial earmarks improve allocation.

Regulatory capture and political economy

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Result Relevance
Stigler (1971) The Theory of Economic Regulation Bell Journal of Economics, 2(1) Regulation captured by regulated industry; agencies serve industry interests over public welfare. If health agencies captured, courts may be second-best corrective.
Laffont & Tirole (1991) The Politics of Government Decision-Making: A Theory of Regulatory Capture QJE, 106(4) Formal model of capture through career concerns and information asymmetry between regulator and politician. Conditions under which external enforcement (courts) improves on captured agencies.
Shleifer & Vishny (1993) Corruption QJE, 108(3) Corruption as distortion of government allocation; organizational structure determines vulnerability. If municipal health allocation is corruption-prone, judicial mandates may improve targeting.

Property rules and liability rules

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Result Relevance
Calabresi & Melamed (1972) Property Rules, Liability Rules, and Inalienability: One View of the Cathedral Harvard Law Review, 85(6) Entitlements protected by property rules (injunctions) vs. liability rules (damages). Property rules efficient when transaction costs low and valuation hard. Court injunctions = property rules; administrative discretion = liability rules. Injunctions efficient only when health loss valuation infeasible.

Procurement theory

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Result Relevance
Laffont & Tirole (1993) A Theory of Incentives in Procurement and Regulation MIT Press Efficient procurement requires competition, commitment, and price discipline. Court-ordered procurement bypasses all three — predicts higher prices, weaker cost revelation, supplier moral hazard.

Litigation theory and selection

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Result Relevance
Shavell (1982) The Social versus the Private Incentive to Bring Suit in a Costly Legal System Journal of Legal Studies, 11(2) Private incentive to sue ≠ social incentive; over-litigation when private gains exceed social benefits. Health litigants capture full treatment benefit but impose diffuse costs on system.
Shavell (1997) The Fundamental Divergence between the Private and the Social Motive to Use the Legal System Journal of Legal Studies, 26(S2) Extended model: private litigation decisions systematically diverge from social optimum. Predicts socially excessive health litigation even when individually rational.
Bebchuk (1984) Litigation and Settlement under Imperfect Information RAND Journal of Economics, 15(3) Under asymmetric information, stronger parties settle advantageously; weaker parties drop claims. Better-resourced plaintiffs (with pharma/legal support) more likely to litigate and win.
Priest & Klein (1984) The Selection of Disputes for Litigation Journal of Legal Studies, 13(1) Litigated cases are selected (close cases go to trial); not representative of underlying disputes. Observed health litigation may not represent the full distribution of unmet health needs.
Galanter (1974) Why the 'Haves' Come Out Ahead: Speculations on the Limits of Legal Change Law & Society Review, 9(1) Repeat players have lower unit costs, learning advantages, strategic behavior; one-shotters disadvantaged. Pharma companies and organized patient groups as repeat players; individual patients as one-shotters. Public defenders may function as repeat players for the poor.

Fiscal federalism and unfunded mandates

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Result Relevance
Oates (1972) Fiscal Federalism Harcourt Brace (book) Fiscal decentralization assigns spending to level with best information, assuming matching of responsibilities to revenues. Courts violate this by imposing federal constitutional obligations on municipal budgets.
Oates (1999) An Essay on Fiscal Federalism JEL, 37(3) Reviews theory and evidence on fiscal decentralization; emphasizes assignment problem and vertical externalities. Framework for understanding why court mandates create vertical fiscal mismatches.
Kornai (1986) The Soft Budget Constraint Kyklos, 39(1) When agents expect bailouts, budget discipline erodes; formalized as expectation of ex post transfers. Reverse SBC: courts impose hard mandates on budgets designed with flexibility. Forward SBC: municipalities may under-invest in delivery if courts will mandate anyway.
Kornai, Maskin & Roland (2003) Understanding the Soft Budget Constraint JEL, 41(4) Comprehensive theoretical treatment and taxonomy of SBC mechanisms. Extended framework applicable to judicial mandate dynamics.

Supplier-induced demand

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Result Relevance
Arrow (1963) Uncertainty and the Welfare Economics of Medical Care AER, 53(5) Health markets fail due to information asymmetry; providers can exploit informational advantage. Foundational: why physician behavior matters for health resource allocation.
Evans (1974) Supplier-Induced Demand: Some Empirical Evidence and Implications in Perlman (ed.), The Economics of Health and Medical Care Physicians shift demand beyond what informed patients would choose; empirical documentation. Classic SID framework applicable to doctor-lawyer-pharma referral chains.
McGuire (2000) Physician Agency Handbook of Health Economics, Vol. 1, Ch. 9 Formal model of physician as imperfect agent; conditions under which inducement occurs. Theoretical backbone for supply-side litigation mechanism.
Dranove (1988) Demand Inducement and the Physician/Patient Relationship Economic Inquiry, 26(2) Tests and refines SID theory; distinguishes inducement from appropriate response to information. Helps distinguish genuine clinical need from supply-driven litigation.

Dynamic commitment and ratchet effects

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Result Relevance
Freixas, Guesnerie & Tirole (1985) Planning under Incomplete Information and the Ratchet Effect Review of Economic Studies, 52(2) Under incomplete information, past performance constrains future flexibility; agents face ratchet. Judicial precedent creates one-way ratchet: once treatment is judicially obtainable, denial becomes harder; mandates accumulate.

Behavioral judicial decision-making

Authors (Year) Title Journal Key Result Relevance
Schelling (1968) The Life You Save May Be Your Own in Chase (ed.), Problems in Public Expenditure Analysis People allocate more to save identified individuals than statistical lives. Judges see the specific patient; statistical lives lost from crowd-out are invisible. Predicts systematic over-granting.
Guthrie, Rachlinski & Wistrich (2001) Inside the Judicial Mind Cornell Law Review, 86 Judges exhibit anchoring, framing effects, and other cognitive biases in controlled experiments. Health case judges anchor on plaintiff's medical prescription; defendant's budget arguments less salient.
Jolls, Sunstein & Thaler (1998) A Behavioral Approach to Law and Economics Stanford Law Review, 50 Integrates behavioral economics with legal analysis; bounded rationality, bounded willpower, bounded self-interest in legal settings. General framework for behavioral analysis of judicial health decisions.
Chen, Moskowitz & Shue (2016) Decision Making Under the Gambler's Fallacy: Evidence from Asylum Judges, Loan Officers, and Baseball Umpires QJE, 131(3) Decision-makers exhibit gambler's fallacy in sequential decisions; prior decisions predict reversals. May generate variation in health case grant rates driven by sequence position rather than case merits.

Gap: These theories generate sharp predictions about efficiency, equity, procurement, fiscal federalism, supply-side dynamics, and judicial behavior that our judge-IV design can test. No existing empirical work systematically maps judicial health mandates to this theoretical framework.
